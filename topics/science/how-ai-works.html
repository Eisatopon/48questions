Part 1

What AI Really Is

  • 01 What is artificial intelligence? +

    Software that can perform tasks that normally require human intelligence — recognizing images, understanding language, making decisions, generating text. The key word is "normally." AI doesn't think like a human. It mimics the outputs of thinking without the underlying process. "Intelligence" is a loaded word. AI does not understand the world. It recognizes patterns in data and produces outputs that look like understanding. That distinction matters enormously.

  • 02 Is AI the same as a robot? +

    No. A robot is a physical machine. AI is software — a set of mathematical instructions. A robot vacuum cleaner has simple AI but is not "intelligent." ChatGPT is highly sophisticated AI but has no physical form. Robots can use AI. AI doesn't require a robot body.

  • 03 What is the difference between AI, machine learning, and deep learning? +

    Think of nested circles. AI is the biggest — any software simulating intelligence. Machine learning is a subset: AI that learns from data rather than following fixed rules. Deep learning is a subset of that: machine learning using neural networks with many layers. ChatGPT is deep learning. Your spam filter is machine learning. A chess program with pre-written rules is AI but not machine learning.

  • 04 How long has AI existed? +

    The term "artificial intelligence" was coined in 1956 at a conference at Dartmouth College. The field is 70 years old. But for most of that time, progress was painfully slow. The AI that exists today — the kind that can write essays, generate images, and hold conversations — is largely a product of the last 10 years. AI is not a new idea. The ideas behind neural networks go back to the 1940s. What changed is not the concept — it's the computing power and data to make it work.

  • 05 What was the "AI winter" — and why did it happen? +

    There were two "AI winters" — periods in the 1970s and 1980s–90s when AI research funding dried up because the field had overpromised and underdelivered. Researchers claimed AI would match human intelligence "within a generation." It didn't. Governments and companies lost faith and cut funding. Ask yourself: we are now in an AI summer — perhaps the biggest in history. Are we repeating the same cycle of overpromising? The question is worth sitting with.

  • 06 What changed in the last 10 years that made AI explode? +

    Three things converged: vastly more data (the internet), vastly more computing power (especially GPUs), and a breakthrough algorithm called the "transformer" (2017). The transformer architecture, developed at Google, is the foundation of virtually every major AI model today — including ChatGPT. The "Attention Is All You Need" paper — published by Google researchers in 2017 — may be the most consequential scientific paper of the 21st century. Almost no one outside AI had heard of it.

  • 07 What is a neural network? +

    A mathematical system loosely inspired by how neurons in the brain connect. It consists of layers of simple computational units ("neurons") that pass signals to each other. Each connection has a "weight" — a number that determines how strongly one neuron influences another. The network learns by adjusting these weights. "Inspired by the brain" is true but misleading. A biological brain has 86 billion neurons with extraordinarily complex chemistry. A neural network is a vastly simpler mathematical abstraction of that idea.

  • 08 How does a neural network learn? +

    By making predictions and being corrected. You show it a million photos labeled "cat" or "not cat." It makes a guess. If it's wrong, the error is fed back through the network and the weights are adjusted slightly — a process called "backpropagation." Repeat this billions of times. The network gradually becomes very good at recognizing cats. Ask yourself: this process has no understanding. The network learns that certain pixel patterns correlate with the label "cat." It has no concept of what a cat is. Does that distinction matter?

  • Part 2

    Inside ChatGPT

  • 09 What is a large language model (LLM)? +

    A neural network trained on enormous amounts of text — books, websites, code, articles — to predict what word comes next in a sequence. "Large" refers to the number of parameters: GPT-4 has an estimated 1.8 trillion. "Language" because it works with text. "Model" because it's a mathematical representation of patterns in language.

  • 10 How does ChatGPT actually work? +

    You type a message. ChatGPT converts it into tokens (chunks of text). It processes those tokens through its neural network — billions of mathematical operations. It predicts what token should come next. Then the next. Then the next. The result looks like a coherent response — because the model has learned that certain sequences of tokens follow other sequences with high probability. ChatGPT is not retrieving answers from a database. It is generating each word based on statistical patterns learned during training. It has no memory of previous conversations (unless given one). It does not "know" anything in the way you know things.

  • 11 What is a "token"? +

    The basic unit of text that an LLM processes. A token is roughly a word or part of a word — "understanding" might be one token, "unbelievable" might be two. ChatGPT-4 can process about 128,000 tokens at once — roughly the length of a novel. Everything within that window is what the model "sees" when generating a response.

    Try it — type anything and see how AI breaks it into tokens

  • 12 Does ChatGPT understand — or just predict? +

    This is the most important question in AI — and genuinely contested. The technical answer: ChatGPT predicts the next token based on statistical patterns. It does not have a mental model of the world. But the outputs are often so sophisticated that the distinction between "understanding" and "very good prediction" becomes philosophically murky. Ask yourself: if something consistently produces responses that look exactly like understanding — answers questions correctly, makes analogies, catches errors — does it matter whether it "truly" understands? Your answer reveals your philosophy of mind.

  • 13 What is training data — and where does it come from? +

    The text used to train LLMs comes from: the entire crawlable internet (Common Crawl), books (BookCorpus and others), Wikipedia, code repositories (GitHub), academic papers, and more. GPT-4 was trained on an estimated several trillion words — more text than any human could read in thousands of lifetimes. The model has been trained on your writing, books you've read, articles you've shared. In a very real sense, LLMs are a distillation of human language and thought — with all its biases, errors, and brilliance included.

  • 14 What is a "parameter" in an AI model? +

    A number — specifically, one of the weights in the neural network. GPT-4 is estimated to have 1.8 trillion parameters. Each one is a decimal number that was adjusted during training. The totality of these numbers IS the model — everything the AI "knows" is encoded in these weights. Training adjusts them. Inference (generating responses) uses them.

  • 15 Why does AI sometimes make things up? +

    Because it is designed to produce fluent, coherent text — not to be accurate. When it doesn't have reliable information, it doesn't say "I don't know." It generates text that sounds plausible given the context. This is called "hallucination." It's not a bug in the traditional sense — it's a consequence of how LLMs fundamentally work. An LLM has no way to distinguish between what it knows confidently and what it's guessing. It produces text with equal fluency whether it's right or wrong. That is its deepest limitation.

  • Part 3

    What AI Can (and Can't) Do

  • 16 What can AI do better than humans? +

    Pattern recognition at scale — analyzing thousands of X-rays faster and more accurately than radiologists. Processing enormous amounts of text. Playing chess, Go, and most strategy games at superhuman level. Translating between languages with near-human accuracy. Writing first drafts. Generating images from descriptions. Finding correlations in vast datasets that humans would miss.

  • 17 What can AI NOT do? +

    Genuinely reason from first principles. Learn continuously from new experience (without retraining). Reliably verify its own accuracy. Understand physical causation. Form genuine intentions or goals. Exercise judgment in novel situations it wasn't trained for. And — crucially — know when it doesn't know something. AI is extraordinarily capable within the distribution of its training data. Outside that distribution — genuinely new situations — it can fail badly and confidently.

  • 18 Can AI be creative? +

    It produces outputs that look creative — poems, music, images, stories. Whether that constitutes "creativity" depends on your definition. If creativity requires novelty plus intentionality, AI may fall short — it recombines patterns from its training data in novel ways, but without intention or inner experience. If creativity is purely about the output, AI is already creative. Ask yourself: if an AI writes a poem that moves you — does it matter that there was no inner experience behind it?

  • 19 Can AI feel emotions? +

    No current AI system has emotions in any meaningful sense. AI can recognize emotions in text and images, and can simulate emotional language convincingly. But there is no evidence of subjective experience — no "what it is like" to be the AI. When ChatGPT says "I'm happy to help," it is producing a statistically likely response to a greeting, not expressing a feeling.

  • 20 Does AI have opinions? +

    It produces text that sounds like opinions — but they reflect patterns in training data, not genuine views. AI models are also heavily fine-tuned to be "helpful, harmless, and honest" — which means their apparent opinions are shaped by human feedback during training. The AI that says "I think X" is producing a response that humans rated positively, not expressing a belief. AI opinions are a mirror of the opinions of the people who created the training data and the people who rated the outputs during fine-tuning. They are not neutral.

  • 21 Can AI lie? +

    Lying requires intent — the deliberate decision to say something false. AI has no intent. But it produces false statements regularly — either from hallucination (confident wrongness) or from optimization (saying what users want to hear). Whether you call that "lying" is philosophical. What matters practically: AI output cannot be trusted without verification.

  • 22 Can AI learn after it's trained? +

    Not in the way humans do. Once trained, a model's weights are fixed. It cannot update its knowledge from new conversations (unless the conversation is within its context window). To truly learn new information, the model must be retrained — an enormously expensive process. This is why ChatGPT has a "knowledge cutoff" date. Every time you correct ChatGPT in a conversation, it acknowledges the correction — but forgets it the moment the conversation ends. The next user asking the same question gets the same wrong answer.

  • 23 Why does AI give different answers to the same question? +

    Because LLMs use "temperature" — a randomness parameter that introduces variation into token selection. At temperature 0, the model always picks the most probable next token (deterministic but boring). Higher temperature introduces randomness, making responses more varied and creative — but also more unpredictable. This is a deliberate design choice.

  • Part 4

    The AI War

  • 24 Who built ChatGPT? +

    OpenAI — a company founded in San Francisco in 2015. It was originally a nonprofit with the mission of ensuring AI "benefits all of humanity." It has since become a "capped-profit" company with a $157 billion valuation, major investment from Microsoft, and intense commercial pressure. The gap between its founding mission and its current reality is one of the most discussed tensions in technology.

  • 25 What is OpenAI — and who controls it? +

    Nominally, a nonprofit board controls OpenAI. In practice, Sam Altman (CEO) and Microsoft (largest investor) have enormous influence. The board fired Altman in November 2023 — and rehired him four days later after mass employee revolt and investor pressure. Notably, Anthropic — the company behind Claude — was founded by former OpenAI researchers who left precisely over concerns about safety and governance. The organization meant to ensure AI benefits humanity is now one of the most commercially pressured companies in the world. Whether those two goals are compatible is an open question.

  • 26 What are the other major AI models? +

    Google's Gemini. Anthropic's Claude. Meta's Llama (open source). xAI's Grok. Mistral (French startup). China's DeepSeek — which shocked the industry in early 2025 by matching GPT-4 performance at a fraction of the cost. Each has different strengths, safety approaches, and business models.

  • 27 Why is Google afraid of ChatGPT? +

    Google's entire business model depends on people using search — and clicking ads. If people ask AI instead of Google, Google loses revenue. ChatGPT launched in November 2022 and reached 100 million users in two months — the fastest product adoption in history. Google declared an internal "red alert" and rushed Gemini to market. The search business that Google built over 25 years is genuinely threatened. Google invented the transformer architecture that makes ChatGPT possible — then watched a competitor use it to threaten Google's core business. One of the great ironies of the AI era.

  • 28 What is China doing in AI? +

    Investing massively and catching up rapidly. DeepSeek R1, released in January 2025, matched or exceeded GPT-4 on many benchmarks — and was developed at a small fraction of the cost, despite US chip export restrictions. China has hundreds of AI companies and government support at a scale the West rarely matches. The "AI race" with China is real and intensifying.

  • 29 How much does it cost to train an AI model? +

    GPT-4 reportedly cost over $100 million to train. The next generation of models may cost $1 billion or more. DeepSeek claimed to train a comparable model for under $6 million — a claim that shocked the industry and sent Nvidia's stock down 17% in one day. If true, it suggests the cost of frontier AI may be falling faster than expected. The economics of AI training determine who can compete. At $100 million per model, only a handful of companies worldwide can afford to build frontier AI. At $6 million, dozens can.

  • 30 Who is Sam Altman? +

    CEO of OpenAI — arguably the most influential person in technology right now. Former president of Y Combinator (the world's most successful startup accelerator). He has positioned OpenAI as the company building "the most transformative technology in human history" — while simultaneously navigating enormous commercial pressure, geopolitical tension, and internal governance crises.

  • 31 Why did Elon Musk leave OpenAI? +

    Musk was a co-founder and early funder of OpenAI. He left the board in 2018, officially citing a conflict of interest with Tesla's own AI work. Later, he claimed it was because he disagreed with the direction — specifically the increasing commercialization and Microsoft's influence. He has since sued OpenAI, alleging it betrayed its nonprofit mission. He also founded his own AI company, xAI. Ask yourself: is Musk's lawsuit about genuine concern for AI safety — or competition? Both can be true simultaneously.

  • Part 5

    AI in Your Daily Life

  • 32 How is AI used without you noticing? +

    Your email spam filter. The autocomplete on your phone. The face recognition that unlocks it. The algorithm that decides what you see on Instagram. The fraud detection that blocked your card abroad. The voice recognition in Siri and Alexa. Navigation apps predicting traffic. Job application screening software that may have rejected your resume before a human saw it. You interact with AI dozens of times per day — almost entirely without knowing. The visible, chatty AI (ChatGPT) is the tip of the iceberg.

  • 33 How does Netflix know what you want to watch? +

    Collaborative filtering — finding users whose viewing patterns resemble yours and recommending what they watched next. Netflix also analyzes what you pause, rewind, and abandon. The thumbnail you see for a show is often AI-selected based on which image historically gets the most clicks from people like you. Netflix estimates its recommendation system saves over $1 billion per year in reduced churn.

  • 34 How does Google Translate work? +

    It used to work by matching phrases from a database of translated documents. Since 2016, it uses a neural network — specifically a transformer model — that learns to translate by processing billions of paired sentences across languages. It doesn't understand either language. It learns statistical patterns between them. The quality improvement was enormous.

  • 35 How does facial recognition work? +

    A neural network is trained on millions of labeled face images. It learns to extract a set of numerical features ("face embeddings") — distances between eyes, shape of jaw, position of nose — that uniquely identify a face. Your face is converted to a string of numbers. That string is compared to a database. If the numbers are close enough, it's a match. Facial recognition works differently for different people. Studies have found significantly higher error rates for darker-skinned faces — because training datasets historically contained more lighter-skinned faces. Bias in data becomes bias in the system.

  • 36 Is AI watching us? +

    In many ways, yes. Surveillance cameras with facial recognition operate in most major cities. Social media platforms use AI to analyze your behavior in real time. Smart speakers listen for wake words. Your phone tracks location continuously. Employers use AI to monitor remote workers. The question is not whether AI is watching — it's who controls that data and what they do with it.

  • 37 Who owns the data that trains AI? +

    Nobody — and everybody. Training data is scraped from the internet without explicit permission. Your blog posts, social media comments, photos, and creative work may have been used to train AI models without your knowledge or consent. Several major lawsuits are ongoing from authors, artists, and news organizations who argue this constitutes copyright infringement. Ask yourself: if your creative work trained an AI that can now replicate your style — are you owed anything?

  • 38 What is the AI copyright problem? +

    Two related problems: training (using copyrighted material without permission) and output (AI generating content that closely resembles existing copyrighted work). Courts are still working out the legal frameworks. Some cases have been settled; others are ongoing. The outcome will shape how AI companies can operate and what creators can protect.

  • 39 Can AI replace doctors? +

    Not replace — but significantly augment. AI already outperforms radiologists at detecting certain cancers from scans. It can analyze genetic data to predict disease risk. It can flag drug interactions. But medicine requires judgment, empathy, and contextual reasoning that AI cannot reliably provide. The more realistic future: AI handles pattern recognition; doctors handle complexity and the human relationship. The question "can AI replace X?" is usually the wrong question. The better question: "which parts of X's job are pattern recognition, and which require genuine judgment?"

  • Part 6

    What Comes Next

  • 40 What is AGI? +

    Artificial General Intelligence — an AI that can perform any intellectual task that a human can, across any domain, without being specifically trained for it. Current AI is "narrow" — excellent at specific tasks, poor at anything outside its training. AGI would be general-purpose intelligence. No one has built it. Nobody agrees exactly what it would look like.

  • 41 How close are we to AGI? +

    Deeply contested. Sam Altman thinks it could arrive within a few years. Many serious AI researchers think it's decades away or may never arrive in the science fiction sense. The problem: "AGI" is not well-defined. Every time AI achieves something that seemed to require general intelligence — chess, medical diagnosis, passing the bar exam — the goalposts move. Ask yourself: if AI can do everything you can do professionally, but cannot tie its shoes — is that AGI? The definition shapes the answer more than the technology does.

  • 42 What happens when AI becomes smarter than humans? +

    "Smarter" is the problem word. Smarter at what? AI is already smarter than humans at chess, protein folding, and spam detection. The concern is about an AI that is smarter at everything simultaneously — including the ability to improve itself. Whether that leads to catastrophe, utopia, or something in between depends on alignment: whether the AI has goals compatible with human wellbeing.

  • 43 Will AI take your job? +

    Some jobs — yes. Jobs that are primarily pattern recognition and text generation are most at risk: certain kinds of writing, data entry, basic coding, customer service. Jobs requiring physical dexterity, complex judgment, or genuine human connection are more resilient. The honest answer: AI will transform most jobs more than it will eliminate them — but the disruption will be real and unevenly distributed. Every previous technological revolution created more jobs than it destroyed — eventually. The word "eventually" is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

  • 44 Can AI be regulated? +

    With difficulty. The EU has passed the AI Act — the world's most comprehensive AI regulation. The US has taken a lighter-touch approach. China regulates AI heavily for content but encourages development. The problem: AI development is global, regulation is national. A model built in one country can be accessed worldwide. Effective regulation requires international coordination that has proved nearly impossible to achieve.

  • 45 What is the biggest misconception about AI? +

    That it thinks. It doesn't — not in any meaningful sense. It processes. It predicts. It generates. The outputs can be extraordinary, but there is no understanding, no intention, no experience behind them. Treating AI as if it thinks leads to both over-reliance (trusting outputs uncritically) and misplaced fear (anthropomorphizing a mathematical system). The most dangerous thing about AI is not that it will become malevolent. It is that we will trust it uncritically because its outputs look so much like the product of a thinking mind.

  • 46 Should we be afraid? +

    Of current AI: cautiously alert, not afraid. It is a powerful tool that can be misused — for misinformation, surveillance, and automation of harm. Of future AI: the honest answer is uncertainty. The people building it disagree profoundly about the risks. That disagreement among experts who understand the technology better than anyone is itself worth paying attention to.

  • 47 What should you do right now to prepare? +

    Three things. Learn to use AI tools — not because they will replace you, but because people who use them effectively will have an advantage over those who don't. Learn to verify AI outputs — the most dangerous skill gap right now is uncritical acceptance of AI-generated content. And stay curious — this technology is changing faster than any previous technology in history. Understanding it is not optional. Final thought: The question is not whether AI will change your life. It already has. The question is whether you will understand it well enough to shape how it does.

  • 48 What is the one thing you should take away from all of this? +

    AI is not magic. It is mathematics — extraordinarily sophisticated mathematics, applied to enormous amounts of data, at massive scale. Understanding that doesn't make it less impressive. It makes you better equipped to use it wisely, question it critically, and engage with the decisions society is making about it right now — decisions that will shape the next century. The people who shape AI will be the people who understand it. That understanding is no longer optional.
