The Hidden Cost of AI:
Energy

Every time you ask ChatGPT a question, something is burning.
Electricity. Water. Carbon.

The AI revolution has a bill — and nobody is talking about who pays it.

Part 1

The Numbers

No questions match your search.

  • 01 How much energy does a single ChatGPT query use? +

    Approximately 10 times more than a Google search. A Google search uses around 0.0003 kilowatt-hours. A ChatGPT query uses roughly 0.001 to 0.01 kilowatt-hours depending on length and complexity. That sounds small — until you multiply it by hundreds of millions of queries per day. There are an estimated 100 million ChatGPT users. If each makes 5 queries a day, that's 500 million queries — consuming roughly the same electricity as a small city, every single day, just for one AI product.

  • 02 How much energy does it take to train a large AI model? +

    Training GPT-3 used approximately 1,300 megawatt-hours of electricity — equivalent to the annual energy consumption of about 120 US homes. GPT-4 is estimated to have used significantly more, though OpenAI has not disclosed the exact figure. Each new generation of models is larger and more expensive to train. Training happens once. But inference — the act of generating responses to queries — happens billions of times. The ongoing energy cost of running AI dwarfs the one-time cost of training it.

  • 03 How much does AI contribute to global electricity demand? +

    The International Energy Agency estimates that data centers — which power AI, cloud computing, and the internet — consumed about 200 terawatt-hours in 2022, roughly 1% of global electricity. By 2026, AI alone could consume as much electricity as the entire country of Japan. Ask yourself: the internet took 30 years to reach this level of energy consumption. AI may match it in five.

  • 04 How much has Google's energy use grown because of AI? +

    Google's total electricity consumption increased by 48% between 2019 and 2023 — almost entirely due to AI. In 2023, Google consumed about 24 terawatt-hours — more than the entire country of Croatia. Google had pledged to run on 100% renewable energy by 2030. That goal is now considered unreachable due to AI growth. Google was once a leader in corporate sustainability. AI has made its own climate commitments impossible to keep. It said so publicly in its 2024 environmental report.

  • 05 How much has Microsoft's energy use grown? +

    Microsoft's carbon emissions increased by 29% between 2020 and 2023 — the opposite direction of its stated climate goals. Microsoft had pledged to be carbon negative by 2030. In 2024, it acknowledged this goal is now in serious jeopardy because of AI data center expansion. Microsoft invested $13 billion in OpenAI. That investment comes with an energy bill that is now threatening its own environmental commitments. The two announcements — the investment and the missed targets — are directly connected.

  • 06 How fast is AI energy consumption growing? +

    Extremely fast. Goldman Sachs estimated in 2024 that data center power demand will grow 160% by 2030. The IEA projects AI could account for up to 10% of global electricity consumption by 2030 — up from less than 1% today. No other technology in history has scaled its energy demand this quickly.

  • 07 What is the carbon footprint of AI? +

    Training GPT-3 produced an estimated 552 tonnes of CO₂ — equivalent to the lifetime emissions of five average cars. But the ongoing inference carbon footprint is far larger. If AI data centers run on fossil fuels, every query contributes to carbon emissions. The carbon footprint depends entirely on where the electricity comes from. An AI query powered by Norwegian hydroelectric is nearly carbon-neutral. The same query powered by a coal plant in Virginia — where many data centers are located — emits real carbon. The technology is the same. The energy source determines everything.

  • 08 Are AI companies transparent about their energy use? +

    No — not fully. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon publish annual sustainability reports, but these rarely break down AI-specific energy consumption separately. OpenAI publishes almost nothing about its environmental impact. The companies that know the most about AI's energy use are the least forthcoming about the details. Ask yourself: if the numbers were good, would companies be reluctant to share them?

  • Part 2

    Why So Much?

  • 09 Why does AI use so much more energy than a regular search? +

    A Google search retrieves and ranks existing documents — it's essentially a sophisticated lookup. An AI query generates a response from scratch — performing billions of mathematical operations across trillions of parameters. The computation required to generate a single coherent paragraph is orders of magnitude greater than finding a webpage. Think of it this way: Google finds the book. AI writes one. Writing requires vastly more work than finding.

  • 10 What are GPUs and why do they matter for energy? +

    GPUs (Graphics Processing Units) are the chips that power AI. Originally designed for video games, they excel at the parallel mathematical operations required for AI training and inference. A single high-end AI GPU — like Nvidia's H100 — consumes up to 700 watts of power. A data center running thousands of these generates enormous heat and requires enormous electricity. Nvidia's H100 chip costs $30,000 and uses as much electricity as a clothes dryer running continuously. Data centers house tens of thousands of them. The business of AI is, in significant part, the business of managing electricity.

  • 11 What is "inference" and why does it matter for energy? +

    Inference is what happens when you actually use an AI — when it generates a response to your query. Training happens once; inference happens every time anyone uses the model. As AI adoption grows, inference becomes the dominant energy cost — not the one-time training run that gets most of the attention.

  • 12 Why are AI models getting bigger — and does bigger mean more energy? +

    Bigger models generally perform better. GPT-2 had 1.5 billion parameters. GPT-3 had 175 billion. GPT-4 is estimated at 1.8 trillion. Each increase in scale requires exponentially more computation to train and more energy to run. The industry has largely operated on the assumption that bigger is better — and has built its infrastructure accordingly. Ask yourself: if smaller models can achieve similar performance at a fraction of the energy cost — as DeepSeek demonstrated in early 2025 — why did the industry default to making everything bigger?

  • 13 What is cooling — and why does it double the energy problem? +

    Data centers generate enormous heat. Keeping servers at safe operating temperatures requires cooling systems that consume almost as much energy as the servers themselves. The "Power Usage Effectiveness" (PUE) ratio measures this: a PUE of 2.0 means that for every watt used by computers, another watt is used for cooling. The best data centers achieve PUE of around 1.1; older ones can be 2.0 or worse.

  • 14 Where are AI data centers located — and why does location matter? +

    Virginia hosts the highest concentration of data centers in the world — attracted by cheap land, tax incentives, and proximity to internet infrastructure. But Virginia's electricity grid relies heavily on natural gas. Data centers in Iceland or Norway run on geothermal and hydroelectric power. The same AI query has a radically different carbon footprint depending on which data center processes it. The tech industry talks about AI as a global, borderless technology. But its energy consumption is intensely physical and local. Where the server sits determines how dirty the computation is.

  • Part 3

    The Comparisons

  • 15 How does AI energy use compare to aviation? +

    Global aviation produces about 2.5% of global CO₂ emissions. AI data centers currently produce less — but are growing far faster. Aviation growth is measured in single-digit percentages per year. AI energy demand is doubling roughly every two years. At current growth rates, AI could match or exceed aviation's carbon footprint within a decade. We have international agreements, carbon taxes, and public outrage about aviation emissions. We have almost no regulatory framework for AI's energy use. One of these industries moves people. The other moves data.

  • 16 How does training one AI model compare to driving a car? +

    Training GPT-3 produced approximately 552 tonnes of CO₂. The average car produces about 4.6 tonnes per year. Training one large AI model is equivalent to running 120 cars for a year — or driving one car for 120 years. And that's one training run of one model, not the ongoing inference cost.

  • 17 How does AI energy use compare to Bitcoin? +

    Bitcoin mining currently consumes about 120-150 terawatt-hours per year — roughly equivalent to Argentina's total electricity consumption. AI data centers already consume more, and are growing faster. Bitcoin's energy use attracted enormous public criticism. AI's energy use has attracted comparatively little, despite being larger. Ask yourself: why does Bitcoin's energy use generate front-page outrage, while AI's larger and faster-growing energy use generates mostly silence?

  • 18 Which countries does AI's energy use compare to? +

    In 2023, data centers consumed roughly 200-250 terawatt-hours — comparable to the entire electricity consumption of Poland or South Africa. By 2030, AI alone could consume 1,000+ terawatt-hours — comparable to Japan or Germany. These are not edge cases. These are among the world's largest economies.

  • 19 How does generating one AI image compare to leaving a light on? +

    Generating a single AI image with a model like DALL-E or Midjourney uses approximately as much electricity as charging a smartphone. That sounds modest — until you consider that billions of AI images are generated every month. The cumulative effect of millions of "small" AI operations adds up to genuinely large numbers. No single query matters. The aggregate of hundreds of millions of queries matters enormously. AI's energy problem is not about individual use — it's about scale.

  • 20 Is AI more or less energy-efficient than human labor for the same task? +

    It depends entirely on the task and scale. For some tasks — analyzing thousands of medical images, translating millions of documents — AI is vastly more efficient than an equivalent human workforce. For tasks that would never have been done at scale (generating millions of marketing emails, creating billions of social media captions), AI creates entirely new energy demand that didn't previously exist. This is the rebound effect: efficiency gains that lead to increased total consumption. AI doesn't replace human energy use — it enables activities at scales humans never attempted.

  • Part 4

    Water & Land

  • 21 Why does AI use water? +

    Data centers generate heat. One of the most effective ways to cool them is evaporative cooling — circulating water through the facility and allowing it to evaporate, carrying heat away. Large data centers can consume millions of liters of fresh water per day. This is not metaphorical — it is literal water drawn from local aquifers, rivers, and municipal supplies.

  • 22 How much water did training GPT-3 use? +

    Researchers estimated that training GPT-3 consumed approximately 700,000 liters of fresh water — enough to fill a small swimming pool. Microsoft reported that its global water consumption increased by 34% between 2021 and 2022, attributing much of this to AI data center growth. 700,000 liters of fresh water to train one AI model. In a world where 2 billion people lack access to safe drinking water, that number deserves more attention than it receives.

  • 23 Where are data centers being built — and what happens to local communities? +

    Data centers are increasingly being built in rural areas — attracted by cheap land, lower property taxes, and proximity to power infrastructure. Local communities often face increased electricity prices as data centers consume large portions of regional grid capacity. In some cases, data centers have drawn down local water tables, affecting agriculture and residential water supply. The communities that host data centers bear the costs — higher electricity prices, water competition, industrial noise — while the benefits (jobs, tax revenue) are often smaller than promised. The economic geography of AI infrastructure is deeply unequal.

  • 24 How much land do AI data centers require? +

    A large hyperscale data center can occupy 100,000 square meters — roughly 14 football fields. Microsoft, Google, and Amazon are building dozens of these simultaneously. The land required for AI infrastructure is modest compared to agriculture or urban development, but the concentration in specific regions creates intense local impacts on power grids, water systems, and real estate.

  • 25 Are nuclear power plants being reopened for AI? +

    Yes. Microsoft signed a 20-year agreement in 2024 to purchase power from the Three Mile Island nuclear plant in Pennsylvania — the same plant that suffered a partial meltdown in 1979 and had been closed since 2019. Google and Amazon have also signed deals to fund new nuclear capacity. The AI industry's energy demand is so large that previously unthinkable energy sources are being revived. The nuclear debate was supposed to be settled. AI has reopened it — not through policy debate, but through purchasing power. A handful of tech companies are effectively determining the future of nuclear energy by signing contracts.

  • 26 What is the "AI energy paradox"? +

    AI is simultaneously one of the largest drivers of new energy demand and one of the most promising tools for optimizing energy systems. AI is being used to improve the efficiency of power grids, optimize renewable energy forecasting, design better batteries, and model climate systems. The technology that is consuming more energy than ever is also the technology best positioned to help us use energy more efficiently. This is not a contradiction — it's a race. Whether AI's contribution to energy efficiency outpaces its contribution to energy consumption is one of the defining questions of the next decade.

  • Part 5

    Who Decides

  • 27 Who is making decisions about AI's energy use? +

    Primarily the companies building AI — Google, Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, OpenAI. Governments have almost no regulatory framework specifically governing AI energy consumption. There are no international agreements, no binding carbon targets for AI companies, and no mandatory disclosure requirements for AI-specific energy use. The industry largely governs itself. The energy decisions being made right now — which power sources to use, where to build data centers, how large to make models — will shape electricity grids and carbon emissions for decades. They are being made by a handful of executives, not by democratic process.

  • 28 Are AI companies using renewable energy? +

    They claim to — but the reality is complex. Tech companies buy renewable energy certificates (RECs), which allow them to claim renewable energy use without necessarily running on renewable energy in real time. The electricity actually powering a data center at any given moment may come from coal or gas, even if the company has purchased equivalent renewable energy certificates elsewhere. Buying a renewable energy certificate in Texas doesn't change what fuels the Virginia power plant running your data center at 2am. "Powered by renewables" is often an accounting statement, not a physical one.

  • 29 What is the role of electricity grids in the AI energy problem? +

    AI data centers require reliable, always-on electricity — they cannot operate on intermittent renewable sources without backup power. This means they often push grids toward fossil fuel backup capacity. In some US regions, utilities have had to delay the retirement of coal plants because new data center demand requires the backup generation capacity. The AI boom may be extending the life of coal plants. The same companies pledging carbon neutrality are, indirectly, keeping fossil fuel infrastructure operational.

  • 30 Do users have any power to change AI's energy footprint? +

    Very little directly. Individual usage choices — avoiding unnecessary AI queries, using smaller models for simple tasks — have negligible impact at scale. The meaningful decisions are made at the infrastructure level: which energy sources power data centers, how efficiently models are designed, and whether regulators impose any constraints. User pressure and public awareness can influence corporate and regulatory decisions, but individual behavioral change is not the lever here. AI energy is a systemic problem that requires systemic solutions. Telling individuals to use less AI is like telling them to recycle during a wildfire — not wrong, but not the intervention that matters.

  • 31 Is there any regulation of AI energy use? +

    Almost none specifically targeting AI. The EU's AI Act, the most comprehensive AI regulation in the world, includes some provisions on energy transparency but no binding limits on energy consumption. The US has no federal AI energy regulation. Some individual states have begun requiring data center energy disclosure. The regulatory vacuum is nearly total.

  • 32 What did the DeepSeek moment reveal about AI energy? +

    In January 2025, Chinese startup DeepSeek released a model matching GPT-4 performance at a fraction of the training cost — reportedly under $6 million versus hundreds of millions for comparable Western models. This demonstrated that the "bigger is always better" assumption was not inevitable. More efficient architectures could dramatically reduce energy consumption without sacrificing performance. If the AI industry had prioritized efficiency from the start — as DeepSeek's results suggest was possible — the energy footprint of AI today would be a fraction of what it is. The energy crisis of AI is partly an engineering choice, not just a physical necessity.

  • Part 6

    What Comes Next

  • 33 Can AI become more energy-efficient? +

    Yes — significantly. Several approaches are showing promise: model distillation (creating smaller models that approximate larger ones), quantization (reducing numerical precision without sacrificing accuracy), neuromorphic chips (hardware that mimics the brain's energy efficiency), and algorithmic improvements that achieve the same results with less computation. The question is whether efficiency improvements will outpace the growth in demand.

  • 34 What are neuromorphic chips? +

    Chips designed to mimic how biological neurons work — processing information through spikes of activity rather than continuous computation. The human brain performs extraordinary cognitive tasks using roughly 20 watts — less than a dim light bulb. Neuromorphic chips attempt to replicate this efficiency. They remain in early development but represent a potential step-change in AI energy efficiency. The human brain runs on 20 watts. The data centers training AI use gigawatts. If neuromorphic computing can close even a fraction of that gap, it would transform the economics and environmental impact of AI entirely.

  • 35 Could nuclear fusion solve AI's energy problem? +

    Potentially — but not soon. Fusion power has been "20 years away" for 60 years. Recent breakthroughs at the National Ignition Facility achieved net energy gain for the first time in 2022. But commercial fusion power is still at minimum a decade away, and AI's energy demand is growing now. Fusion is a long-term possibility, not a near-term solution.

  • 36 Is AI being used to solve its own energy problem? +

    Yes — with some genuine results. Google's DeepMind used AI to reduce the cooling energy of its own data centers by 40%. AI is being applied to optimize renewable energy grid management, predict energy demand, improve battery storage design, and accelerate materials discovery for solar panels. The technology is being used to address the problems it creates. Ask yourself: if AI can reduce its own data center cooling costs by 40%, why isn't every data center doing this immediately? The answer tells you something about how slowly efficiency improvements spread even when the technology exists.

  • 37 What is "Jevons paradox" — and does it apply to AI? +

    Jevons paradox states that when technology makes a resource more efficient to use, total consumption of that resource often increases — because efficiency makes it cheaper and more accessible. The steam engine became more efficient, but total coal consumption exploded. Cars became more fuel-efficient, but total fuel consumption increased. AI is becoming more efficient per query — but the number of queries is growing so fast that total energy use continues to rise. Efficiency without limits produces more consumption, not less. This is why "AI is getting more efficient" is not, by itself, a satisfying answer to the energy problem.

  • 38 Will AI accelerate or slow climate change? +

    Genuinely uncertain — and the answer depends entirely on choices not yet made. AI could accelerate materials science breakthroughs that enable cheaper solar and better batteries. It could optimize global energy grids for maximum renewable integration. Or it could consume enormous quantities of fossil fuel-generated electricity while these applications remain theoretical. The technology itself is neutral. The energy source and application determine the outcome.

  • 39 What does "carbon negative" mean — and can AI companies achieve it? +

    Carbon negative means removing more carbon from the atmosphere than you emit. Microsoft pledged to be carbon negative by 2030. Google pledged net zero by 2030. Both companies' emissions have increased since making these pledges — driven primarily by AI. Both have acknowledged their targets are now in serious jeopardy. "Carbon negative" is currently a statement of intent, not trajectory.

  • 40 What would meaningful regulation of AI energy look like? +

    Several approaches are being discussed: mandatory energy disclosure for AI training runs above a certain size, carbon pricing that forces AI companies to internalize their emissions, requirements that new data centers use a minimum percentage of genuinely renewable energy, and efficiency standards for AI models similar to fuel efficiency standards for cars. None of these exist yet at meaningful scale anywhere in the world. We have fuel efficiency standards for cars, energy ratings for appliances, and carbon taxes in dozens of countries. We have almost no equivalent for the fastest-growing energy consumer in modern history. That gap is a policy choice.

  • 41 Should some AI applications be restricted on energy grounds? +

    This is an emerging debate. Some argue that AI applications with high energy cost and low social value — generating billions of marketing images, powering recommendation algorithms — should face energy taxes or restrictions. Others argue that market forces should determine which applications are worth the energy cost. No government has yet restricted any AI application on energy grounds. Ask yourself: we restrict cars from certain areas to reduce pollution. We tax carbon to reduce emissions. Why is AI the one technology where the environmental cost generates almost no policy response?

  • 42 What is the most important thing most people don't know about AI and energy? +

    That the decisions being made right now — by a handful of companies, largely without democratic oversight — will determine the energy infrastructure of the 21st century. The data centers being built today will run for 20-30 years. The power purchase agreements being signed today will shape electricity grids for decades. The energy future of AI is being decided in boardrooms, not parliaments. And most people have no idea it's happening.

  • 43 Is the energy cost of AI worth it? +

    For some applications — almost certainly yes. AI-assisted drug discovery, climate modeling, medical diagnosis, and scientific research represent genuine value that may justify significant energy expenditure. For others — generating marketing copy, making chatbots more engaging, optimizing advertising targeting — the calculus is far less clear. The problem is that no one is making this distinction. All AI energy use is treated equally, regardless of what it produces. Not all electricity is equal — we don't power hospitals and billboards with the same moral weight. Not all AI queries are equal either. The energy debate needs to ask not just "how much?" but "for what?"

  • 44 What should you take away from all of this? +

    AI's energy consumption is real, large, and growing faster than almost any technology in history. The companies driving it have made environmental commitments they are now breaking. The regulatory frameworks to govern it barely exist. The decisions being made now will shape electricity grids and carbon emissions for decades. And the technology responsible for this — the same technology consuming the energy — may also be our most powerful tool for solving the problem it is creating. That contradiction is not a reason for despair. It is a reason for paying attention. Final thought: every major technology of the 20th century — cars, planes, plastics — created environmental consequences that took decades to recognize and longer to address. We are at the beginning of AI's environmental story, not the end. The question is whether we will wait decades again, or act while the infrastructure is still being built.
