Part 1

The Basics

No questions match your search.

  • 01 What exactly is the China-Taiwan conflict? +

    A dispute over the political status of Taiwan — a self-governing island of 23 million people located 180 kilometers off the coast of mainland China. China claims Taiwan as part of its sovereign territory and has never renounced the use of military force to bring about reunification. Taiwan has governed itself with its own military, elections, currency, and foreign policy since 1949, and most of its population does not identify as Chinese. Most countries formally recognize China's position while maintaining unofficial relations with Taiwan. The result is a decades-long standoff with no clear resolution. Taiwan is not officially recognized as an independent country by most governments — including the United States. Yet it functions as one in almost every practical sense. This ambiguity is not an accident. It is the architecture of the current peace.

  • 02 Is Taiwan a country? +

    It depends on who you ask — and the answer carries enormous political consequences. Taiwan has all the functional attributes of a state: a defined territory, a permanent population, an elected government, and the capacity to conduct foreign relations. It issues its own passports, fields its own military, and runs its own economy. But only 12 countries formally recognize it as a sovereign state. Most nations recognize the People's Republic of China instead — and officially treat Taiwan as part of China, even while maintaining substantial unofficial ties. Taiwan competes in the Olympics under the name "Chinese Taipei" and cannot use its name or flag at most international events. This is not a technicality. It is the daily reality of one of the world's most complex political situations.

  • 03 What is the "One China" policy? +

    The diplomatic framework under which most countries — including the United States — formally recognize that there is "one China" and that the government of the People's Republic of China is the legitimate government of China. Crucially, the US policy "acknowledges" (not "accepts") Beijing's position that Taiwan is part of China — deliberately ambiguous language negotiated in the 1970s. Both China and Taiwan historically claimed to be the sole legitimate government of all of China. Today, Taiwan's government has largely moved away from this claim, while China's has not. The word "acknowledges" in the 1972 Shanghai Communiqué was chosen with extreme care. It allowed the US to maintain relations with both Beijing and Taipei without explicitly accepting China's sovereignty claim over Taiwan. That single word has been the foundation of cross-strait stability for 50 years.

  • 04 What do the people of Taiwan actually want? +

    Surveys consistently show that a large majority of Taiwanese people prefer to maintain the current ambiguous status quo — neither formal independence nor reunification. Support for immediate independence is a minority position; support for immediate reunification is even smaller. However, Taiwanese identity — as distinct from Chinese identity — has been rising steadily for decades. Younger Taiwanese overwhelmingly identify as Taiwanese rather than Chinese. The political direction of Taiwan's democracy has consistently moved away from Beijing, not toward it. In 1992, roughly 17% of Taiwanese identified exclusively as Taiwanese. By 2024, that figure exceeded 60%. This shift in identity is perhaps the most consequential long-term development in cross-strait relations — and it is happening regardless of what any government decides.

  • 05 Why hasn't this been resolved? +

    Because neither side can accept the other's terms — and the status quo, while unsatisfying, has been tolerable for all major parties. China will not accept Taiwanese independence. Taiwan will not accept reunification on terms that eliminate its democracy and autonomy. The US will not abandon Taiwan but will not formally recognize its independence either. This triangle of incompatible positions has produced a frozen conflict that has lasted 75 years — maintained not by agreement, but by mutual deterrence and shared economic interest.

  • 06 Where is Taiwan — and why does its location matter? +

    Taiwan sits at the center of what strategists call the "first island chain" — a string of islands running from Japan through Taiwan to the Philippines that forms a geographic barrier between China and the open Pacific. For China, Taiwan in unfriendly hands blocks naval access to the Pacific. For the US and its allies, Taiwan anchors the strategic architecture that has contained Chinese military expansion since 1945. Its location makes it far more than an island — it is a pivot point for the entire Indo-Pacific balance of power. The Taiwan Strait is one of the world's busiest shipping lanes. About 50% of global container shipping and 88% of the world's largest ships pass through it. A conflict would not only be a military crisis — it would immediately become an economic one for the entire planet.

  • 07 How large and capable is Taiwan's military? +

    Taiwan has a military of approximately 165,000 active personnel, with reservists numbering in the hundreds of thousands. It fields modern fighter jets (F-16s), submarines, missiles, and a growing arsenal of asymmetric weapons designed to make any Chinese invasion extremely costly. Taiwan has invested heavily in "porcupine" strategy — making itself difficult and painful to attack rather than trying to match China's military symmetrically. Its navy and air force would be heavily outgunned by China's in a prolonged conventional war, but the goal is to survive long enough for deterrence to work.

  • 08 What is the Taiwan Relations Act? +

    A 1979 US law passed after Washington switched diplomatic recognition from Taiwan to the People's Republic of China. The TRA requires the US to provide Taiwan with defensive arms, maintain the capacity to resist any resort to force that would jeopardize Taiwan's security, and treat threats to Taiwan as matters of "grave concern." It does not require the US to militarily defend Taiwan — but it creates strong political and legal pressure to do so. It is the legal foundation of the US-Taiwan unofficial relationship. The TRA was designed to be deliberately ambiguous about US military commitments to Taiwan. Every US president since Carter has been asked directly whether the US would defend Taiwan militarily. Most have given ambiguous answers. President Biden gave clear answers multiple times — and was walked back by the White House each time.

  • Part 2

    The History — and Why It Still Matters Today

  • 09 How did Taiwan become separate from mainland China? +

    Through a civil war that ended in 1949. China's Nationalist government (the Kuomintang, or KMT) and the Communist Party of China (CPC) had been fighting, with interruptions, since the 1920s. After World War II, the civil war resumed. In 1949, the Communists under Mao Zedong won, established the People's Republic of China on the mainland, and the Nationalists under Chiang Kai-shek retreated to Taiwan with approximately 1.2 million soldiers and civilians — along with China's gold reserves and many of its cultural treasures. Both governments claimed to be the legitimate government of all of China. The government that retreated to Taiwan still called itself the "Republic of China" — the name it had held since the fall of the Qing Dynasty in 1912. For decades, it held China's seat at the United Nations and was recognized by most Western nations as the real China. That changed in 1971, when the UN voted to give the seat to the People's Republic.

  • 10 Was Taiwan always Chinese? +

    The history is more complicated than either side usually acknowledges. Taiwan's indigenous population has lived there for thousands of years. Han Chinese migrants began settling in significant numbers in the 17th century. The Dutch colonial period lasted from 1624 to 1662. Taiwan was under Qing Dynasty (Chinese) rule from 1683 to 1895 — when it was ceded to Japan following China's defeat in the First Sino-Japanese War. Japan ruled Taiwan until 1945. The ROC government took control after Japan's surrender. So Taiwan was under Qing Chinese rule for about 212 years, Japanese rule for 50 years, and the Republic of China for about 80 years. Beijing emphasizes the Qing-era connection to establish historical ownership. Taiwan's independence advocates emphasize that the People's Republic of China has never governed Taiwan for a single day. Both facts are true — and they lead to very different conclusions about what "belongs" to whom.

  • 11 What was the 1992 Consensus — and why does it still matter? +

    A purported agreement between Beijing and Taipei in 1992 that both sides accept there is "one China" — but each side is free to define what "one China" means. For Beijing, it means the PRC. For the KMT in Taiwan, it historically meant the ROC. This ambiguity allowed cross-strait dialogue and economic relations to flourish in the 2000s and early 2010s. The problem: Taiwan's Democratic Progressive Party (DPP), which has governed Taiwan since 2016, disputes that the 1992 Consensus was ever agreed, or that it should be binding. Without a shared framework, direct political dialogue between Taipei and Beijing has essentially ceased. The 1992 Consensus was never a written agreement — it was a verbal understanding whose terms both sides interpreted differently from the start. Its collapse as a diplomatic framework illustrates how much cross-strait stability depended on deliberate ambiguity that becomes harder to maintain as Taiwan's identity shifts.

  • 12 What happened in 1996 — and why does it matter today? +

    In 1995–1996, as Taiwan prepared for its first direct presidential election, China conducted military exercises including missile tests in waters near Taiwan — an attempt to intimidate voters. The US responded by sending two aircraft carrier battle groups to the Taiwan Strait — the most direct US military signal to China in decades. China backed down. Taiwan's election proceeded. This "Third Taiwan Strait Crisis" established that the US was willing to intervene militarily, and drove China to begin the massive military modernization it has pursued for the past 30 years — specifically designed to prevent the US from repeating that intervention. The 1996 crisis is the pivot point of modern cross-strait military history. China's humiliation when confronted with US carriers convinced its leadership that it could not credibly threaten Taiwan until its military could deter or defeat US intervention. That calculation has driven 30 years of Chinese military spending — and the situation is now far more balanced than in 1996.

  • 13 How has Taiwan's democracy changed the conflict? +

    Profoundly. When Taiwan was an authoritarian state under KMT rule, reunification was theoretically imaginable — two governments could negotiate a settlement. A democratic Taiwan is different: any agreement with Beijing must survive a popular vote or referendum. Taiwan's democracy has produced a population that increasingly identifies as Taiwanese rather than Chinese, and consistently elects governments skeptical of Beijing. China cannot negotiate Taiwan's future without negotiating with Taiwan's people — and Taiwan's people have made their preferences clear. Taiwan's democracy is one of the most robust in Asia by international rankings. Its contrast with Hong Kong — where China's "one country, two systems" promise has been effectively dismantled — has made the prospect of reunification dramatically less appealing to Taiwanese voters than it was 30 years ago.

  • 14 What happened in Hong Kong — and how has it affected Taiwan? +

    Hong Kong was returned to China by Britain in 1997 under a "one country, two systems" arrangement that was supposed to preserve its freedoms for 50 years. By 2020, China had imposed a National Security Law that effectively ended Hong Kong's political autonomy, jailed opposition politicians, and dismantled the free press. From Taiwan's perspective, "one country, two systems" has been a broken promise — a demonstration of what reunification would actually mean. Support for reunification in Taiwan polls, which was never high, has collapsed since 2019. Beijing proposed "one country, two systems" as the framework for Taiwan's reunification. Hong Kong showed what that framework looks like in practice. Taiwan's voters noticed. The political cost of Hong Kong's dismantling for Beijing's Taiwan strategy was enormous — and entirely self-inflicted.

  • 15 Has there been military conflict between China and Taiwan before? +

    Yes — most significantly in the 1950s. The First Taiwan Strait Crisis (1954–1955) involved Chinese shelling of Taiwan-held islands and a US commitment to defend Taiwan. The Second Taiwan Strait Crisis (1958) saw China bombard the island of Quemoy for 44 days. Taiwan and China both held small islands near the mainland throughout the Cold War — some contested by artillery fire for decades. Since the 1990s, the conflict has been conducted through political pressure, economic coercion, military exercises, and disinformation rather than direct fire.

  • 16 Why does history remain so central to this conflict — and not just as background? +

    Because both sides' present-day positions rest on competing historical claims. China's claim to Taiwan is legitimized by the argument that Taiwan was historically part of China and that the PRC is the successor state to all of Chinese history. Taiwan's case for self-determination rests on the argument that it has governed itself separately for longer than many countries have existed, and that its people constitute a distinct political community. Neither claim is internally wrong — they simply lead to incompatible conclusions. History here is not background; it is the argument. Chinese schoolchildren are taught that Taiwan is a "sacred" part of Chinese territory whose return is a matter of national dignity. Taiwanese schoolchildren increasingly learn a history centered on Taiwan itself rather than mainland China. These are not merely educational differences — they are two populations being prepared for two different futures.

  • Part 3

    Why Taiwan Matters

  • 17 What are semiconductors — and why does Taiwan dominate them? +

    Semiconductors are the chips that power every electronic device — smartphones, computers, cars, military systems, medical equipment, data centers. Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (TSMC), based in Taiwan, manufactures roughly 60% of the world's semiconductors and over 90% of the most advanced chips — those at 5 nanometers and below. No other country or company can currently replicate TSMC's capabilities. The US, Europe, Japan, and China are all spending billions trying to build domestic chip industries — but building chip fabrication capacity takes a decade and costs hundreds of billions. TSMC's most advanced chips are produced in a single cluster of facilities in Hsinchu, Taiwan. A conflict that damaged or disrupted those facilities would cause an immediate global economic crisis. Cars, smartphones, servers, military equipment — virtually everything with a circuit board would be affected within months. This is not hyperbole. It is supply chain arithmetic.

  • 18 How large is Taiwan's economy? +

    Taiwan has a GDP of approximately $750 billion (2024) — comparable to the Netherlands or Saudi Arabia. It is the world's 21st largest economy. But its economic importance far exceeds its GDP because of its position in global supply chains. Taiwan is one of the world's largest exporters of electronics and technology components. Its per capita income exceeds that of South Korea, Japan, and most of Europe. It has achieved this through a combination of manufacturing excellence, education investment, and strategic positioning in global technology supply chains.

  • 19 Would China benefit economically from controlling Taiwan? +

    In theory, yes — but the reality is more complicated. China would gain TSMC's manufacturing capacity, Taiwan's technological expertise, and its geographic position. But TSMC's value depends on its talent, its supply chains, its relationships with Western companies, and its access to cutting-edge equipment — all of which would be severely disrupted by a military takeover. Western sanctions following an invasion would likely cut off TSMC from the equipment and customers it needs. The chip industry's value cannot simply be seized — it runs on people and relationships that a military occupation would destroy. TSMC's founder, Morris Chang, has publicly stated that TSMC would be "not operable" in the event of a Chinese invasion. The plants could be physically seized, but the knowledge, talent, and supply chains that make them function would leave. This is one reason some analysts believe China would prefer political coercion to military conquest.

  • 20 Why are countries like Japan and South Korea so concerned about Taiwan? +

    For multiple reasons. Japan's southernmost islands are close to Taiwan, and a Chinese-controlled Taiwan would put Chinese military forces directly adjacent to Japanese territory. South Korea's semiconductor industry competes with Taiwan but also relies on the same global supply chains. Both countries are US treaty allies whose security architectures assume American dominance of the western Pacific — a dominance that a Chinese takeover of Taiwan would fundamentally challenge. Both countries also remember what Chinese regional hegemony has historically looked like and have strong incentives to prevent it. Japan's former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe said explicitly that "a Taiwan emergency is a Japanese emergency." Japan has significantly increased its defense spending and military cooperation with the US since 2022, partly in response to the Taiwan situation and partly in response to Russia's invasion of Ukraine — which many Japanese officials see as a preview of what China might attempt.

  • 21 What would a conflict over Taiwan cost the global economy? +

    Estimates vary widely but consistently reach into the trillions. A 2023 Bloomberg Economics analysis estimated a full-scale conflict could cost the global economy $10 trillion — roughly 10% of global GDP — in the first year alone. This includes disruption to semiconductor supply chains, collapse of trade through the Taiwan Strait, economic sanctions and counter-sanctions between major powers, and the direct destruction of infrastructure. These figures dwarf the costs of any recent conflict. Taiwan is too economically interconnected for any conflict involving it to remain contained.

  • 22 What is the "silicon shield" — and how real is it? +

    The theory that Taiwan's semiconductor dominance protects it from Chinese military action — because attacking Taiwan would destroy the chips that China's own economy desperately needs. China is the world's largest consumer of semiconductors. Disrupting Taiwan's chip production would also devastate Chinese industry. The "silicon shield" has genuine logic: mutual economic destruction is a deterrent. But critics point out that China has been aggressively building its own domestic chip industry, and that as China becomes more self-sufficient in chips, the deterrent value of Taiwan's semiconductor dominance may decline. China's semiconductor self-sufficiency is a race against time. The more capable China's domestic chip industry becomes, the less constrained it is by the silicon shield. This is why the US has imposed export controls on advanced chip technology to China — attempting to keep China dependent on Taiwan's production for longer.

  • 23 What does Taiwan mean symbolically — beyond economics and strategy? +

    Taiwan is one of the most successful democracies in Asia. It has a free press, competitive elections, rule of law, and high living standards — achieved despite constant pressure from an authoritarian neighbor that claims it as its own. For advocates of democratic governance, Taiwan represents proof that Chinese culture and democracy are compatible — a direct challenge to Beijing's argument that its authoritarian model is culturally appropriate. For China's leadership, Taiwan's existence as a thriving democracy within what it claims as its own territory is both an embarrassment and a implicit critique of the mainland's political system.

  • 24 Is China already waging economic and political warfare against Taiwan? +

    Yes — continuously. China uses trade restrictions (banning Taiwanese agricultural products, restricting tourism), diplomatic pressure (blocking Taiwan from international organizations, pressuring countries to downgrade relations), military coercion (regular incursions into Taiwan's Air Defense Identification Zone, naval exercises simulating blockades), disinformation campaigns, and political influence operations targeting Taiwanese elections. This "grey zone" warfare falls below the threshold of open armed conflict while continuously pressuring Taiwan and testing its resilience. China's military aircraft entered Taiwan's Air Defense Identification Zone (ADIZ) over 2,000 times in 2023. This is a sustained campaign of pressure designed to normalize Chinese military presence around Taiwan, exhaust Taiwan's air force, and signal capability without triggering a military response. It is warfare by other means.

  • Part 4

    What China Wants — and What It Believes Is at Stake

  • 25 What does China's leadership actually want from Taiwan? +

    Reunification — the incorporation of Taiwan under Chinese sovereignty. Xi Jinping has called this a "historic mission" that cannot be "passed on from generation to generation." China's official position is that reunification is inevitable, that it prefers peaceful means, but has never renounced military force. Beyond the stated position, China's motivations include completing what it considers the unfinished business of the civil war, eliminating a US-aligned military presence off its coast, gaining control of Taiwan's semiconductor industry, and cementing the legitimacy of the Communist Party's claim to represent all of China. The Chinese Communist Party's claim to legitimacy rests significantly on nationalism and the narrative of restoring China to greatness after the "century of humiliation." Taiwan — a territory claimed as Chinese but not controlled — represents an unhealed wound in that narrative. For Xi Jinping personally, Taiwan reunification would be a historical achievement on par with anything since Mao. This is not just policy. It is identity.

  • 26 What does China believe is at stake beyond territory? +

    Legitimacy, sovereignty, and the credibility of its political system. A Taiwan that remains permanently independent — especially one that became formally recognized — would be interpreted by China's leadership as a fundamental defeat: proof that the Communist Party cannot achieve its core national mission. It would also signal to other regions with separatist movements (Tibet, Xinjiang) that resistance has a future. And it would demonstrate that the US can successfully challenge Chinese claims at China's doorstep — which China views as existential to its great-power status. From Beijing's perspective, allowing Taiwan to drift permanently beyond its reach is not just a territorial loss. It is a demonstration that China is not, in fact, a great power capable of enforcing its core interests. The strategic implication is that every adversary — from the South China Sea claimants to India — would interpret Chinese weakness over Taiwan as license to test its limits elsewhere.

  • 27 Does China have a deadline for reunification? +

    Not officially — but Xi Jinping has linked reunification to the "great rejuvenation of the Chinese nation" and to the centenary of the People's Republic in 2049. US military officials, including former Indo-Pacific Command chief Admiral Philip Davidson, have warned of Chinese military readiness to act by 2027 — the centenary of the People's Liberation Army. These are assessments of capability, not confirmed intentions. Chinese military and political timelines are closely held. What is clear is that Xi has signaled urgency — and that the window for peaceful resolution, if it ever existed, is not expanding.

  • 28 How has Xi Jinping changed China's approach to Taiwan? +

    Significantly. Under previous leaders, China's approach emphasized patience and economic integration — the theory that growing economic ties would make Taiwan increasingly dependent on and compatible with the mainland. Xi has shifted toward more assertive military signaling, reduced dialogue, and explicit statements linking his personal legacy to reunification. Under Xi, China has eliminated the semi-autonomous political space in Hong Kong, increased military pressure on Taiwan, and adopted a more confrontational posture toward the United States. The era of "hide and bide" — Deng Xiaoping's instruction to conceal capabilities and avoid confrontation — appears to be over. Xi Jinping has consolidated personal power to a degree not seen since Mao. This matters for Taiwan: decisions about whether and when to act militarily are now concentrated in a smaller group of decision-makers, with fewer institutional checks. Personalized systems are harder to predict — and harder to deter through institutional signals.

  • 29 Does Chinese public opinion support military action against Taiwan? +

    Surveys conducted within China — which must be interpreted cautiously given constraints on free expression — suggest strong nationalist sentiment about Taiwan and broad acceptance of the principle that Taiwan should eventually return to China. However, support for military action that would bring economic costs to ordinary Chinese people is less clear. China's middle class has grown wealthy during decades of stability and economic integration with the world. A war that disrupted that would be deeply unpopular. Whether public opinion acts as a meaningful constraint on the Communist Party's decisions is uncertain.

  • 30 How does Russia's invasion of Ukraine affect China's calculations about Taiwan? +

    In multiple ways, some encouraging action and some discouraging it. The sanctions response to Russia demonstrated that economic costs of military aggression can be severe. Russia's military difficulties showed that even a major military power can struggle against a determined smaller defender. These lessons likely gave Chinese planners pause. At the same time, the Ukraine war demonstrated that the West's response, while substantial, stopped short of direct military intervention. China may have drawn the lesson that the US and Europe will support a defender with weapons and sanctions — but not necessarily with troops. The most important lesson China appears to have drawn from Ukraine is about speed. Russia's slow, grinding campaign gave the West time to organize and Ukraine time to adapt. A successful operation against Taiwan, from China's perspective, would need to be fast enough that the US cannot intervene effectively. This "fait accompli" logic drives Chinese military planning toward rapid, decisive operations rather than attritional war.

  • 31 What would China actually offer Taiwan in a reunification deal? +

    China's official offer is "one country, two systems" — the same framework applied to Hong Kong, which promised to preserve Hong Kong's freedoms for 50 years. Taiwan would theoretically retain its own government, laws, military, and way of life under Chinese sovereignty. Given what happened to Hong Kong, this offer has virtually no credibility in Taiwan. Chinese officials have occasionally floated more generous terms — including allowing Taiwan to keep its military — but these have not been formalized, and Chinese nationalism makes offering genuine autonomy politically difficult domestically.

  • 32 Is there any scenario in which China achieves reunification peacefully? +

    Theoretically, but it would require political changes in both Taiwan and China that currently seem unlikely. Taiwan's electorate would need to vote for a party willing to negotiate reunification on terms that include accepting Chinese sovereignty — something no major Taiwanese party currently advocates. China would need to offer credible, verifiable guarantees of genuine autonomy — which its record in Hong Kong makes nearly impossible to establish. The gap between what China is willing to offer and what Taiwan would require to voluntarily accept reunification has widened, not narrowed, over the past 30 years.

  • Part 5

    What the US & the World Do

  • 33 What is "strategic ambiguity" — and why does the US use it? +

    The deliberate US policy of not specifying exactly what it would do if China attacked Taiwan. The US sells Taiwan defensive weapons, maintains unofficial relations, and has strong political commitments to Taiwan's security — but has no formal treaty obligation to defend it. This ambiguity is carefully constructed: it deters China (which cannot be certain the US would stay out of a conflict) while also deterring Taiwan (which cannot be certain the US would intervene if it provoked a confrontation by declaring independence). It has maintained peace for 50 years — but critics argue it is becoming less credible as China's military grows stronger. Strategic ambiguity works by imposing risk on both sides. The problem is that it becomes less effective as one side grows confident enough to test it. As China's military capabilities improve relative to the US, the ambiguity that deters China may erode — unless reinforced by clearer signals of US commitment. This is the central tension in current US Taiwan policy.

  • 34 Would the US actually fight to defend Taiwan? +

    Unknown — and that uncertainty is the point of strategic ambiguity. President Biden said clearly on four separate occasions that the US would defend Taiwan militarily. Each time, the White House walked back his statement, reaffirming the traditional "strategic ambiguity" policy. The gap between what presidents say and what the official policy is reveals genuine internal debate within the US government. What is clear: a Chinese military takeover of Taiwan would be catastrophic for US credibility and strategic position in Asia. What is less clear is whether the US would risk a direct military conflict with a nuclear-armed China to prevent it. The US has never explicitly tested the question of whether it would fight a nuclear power in direct combat to defend a non-treaty ally. That question — and the uncertainty about the answer — is the foundation of both deterrence and risk in the Taiwan Strait.

  • 35 What weapons does the US sell Taiwan — and why does it matter? +

    The US has sold Taiwan billions of dollars in military equipment including F-16 fighter jets, Patriot missile systems, Harpoon anti-ship missiles, Stinger anti-aircraft missiles, and other defensive systems. These sales are authorized under the Taiwan Relations Act. They serve multiple purposes: strengthening Taiwan's ability to resist Chinese military pressure, signaling US commitment to Taiwan's security, and maintaining a level of deterrence that does not require direct US military intervention. China protests each sale but has been unable to stop them.

  • 36 What role does Japan play? +

    A crucial and increasingly active one. Japan has a mutual defense treaty with the US and hosts major US military bases. Japan's closest territory is less than 200km from Taiwan. Under its revised national security strategy adopted in 2022, Japan significantly increased defense spending and acquired long-range strike capabilities for the first time since World War II. Japanese officials have been explicit that a Taiwan contingency would have direct implications for Japanese security. In any US military response to a Chinese attack on Taiwan, Japan's bases and cooperation would likely be essential — which makes Japan a de facto stakeholder in Taiwan's defense.

  • 37 What does the EU do — and what could it do? +

    The EU has no formal security commitment to Taiwan but has increasingly vocal political support for the status quo. European countries are Taiwan's major chip customers and would be severely affected by a conflict. The EU's leverage is primarily economic — it could impose sanctions on China following a military action, which would be significantly more damaging than US sanctions alone given China's trade dependence on Europe. However, European unity on China policy is inconsistent, and several European nations maintain strong economic ties with Beijing that complicate coordinated responses.

  • 38 What are US chip export controls — and how do they relate to Taiwan? +

    Since 2022, the US has imposed sweeping export controls preventing advanced semiconductor equipment and chips from being sold to China — including by non-US companies using US technology. The stated goal is to prevent China from developing advanced military chips domestically. The effect is to slow China's semiconductor self-sufficiency and maintain its dependence on Taiwan's production. This is economic warfare in the service of Taiwan strategy: keeping China needing what Taiwan produces, making the cost of attacking Taiwan higher, and preventing China from building the technological base for advanced military systems. These export controls are the most significant US economic intervention against China since the Cold War. They also represent a fundamental reversal of decades of US policy that assumed economic engagement would moderate Chinese behavior. The chips war and the Taiwan question are the same question — approached from two different directions.

  • 39 What is TSMC doing to reduce Taiwan's concentration risk? +

    Under pressure from the US and other governments, TSMC is building major fabrication plants in Arizona (US), Japan, and potentially Germany. These facilities will manufacture some advanced chips outside Taiwan. However, TSMC's most cutting-edge production — the 2nm and below nodes — will remain in Taiwan for the foreseeable future. The geographic diversification is real but limited: it reduces but does not eliminate the world's dependence on Taiwan for the most advanced chips. The plants being built abroad will take years to reach full production and will not match Taiwan's leading-edge capabilities for a decade or more.

  • 40 Is deterrence working? +

    So far — there has been no military conflict. But deterrence is inherently difficult to assess because absence of conflict is not proof that deterrence caused it. China may not have attacked Taiwan because it couldn't, because it didn't want to, or because deterrence worked. The balance of military power has shifted toward China since the 1990s. US military exercises modeling a Taiwan conflict have produced mixed results. The deterrence equation is changing — and the question of whether it remains adequate is the most important security question in the Indo-Pacific.

  • Part 6

    What Could Happen: Scenarios, Not Predictions

  • 41 Scenario: The status quo continues indefinitely +

    The most likely scenario in the near term — and what has happened for 75 years. Taiwan continues to govern itself, China continues to claim it, the US continues to maintain strategic ambiguity, and no one forces a resolution. In this scenario, the conflict is managed rather than resolved. Economic integration remains significant despite political tensions. Military pressure from China continues at a high but sub-conflict level. Taiwan's identity continues to diverge from mainland China. The status quo persists not because anyone is satisfied, but because the costs of changing it remain too high for all parties. The status quo is not stable in the long run — it is a temporary equilibrium maintained by deterrence and mutual economic interest. The forces eroding it (Chinese military growth, Taiwanese identity shift, US-China strategic competition) are all moving in directions that make indefinite continuation less likely over decades than over years.

  • 42 Scenario: China imposes a blockade +

    China surrounds Taiwan with naval and air forces, cutting off shipping and trade — attempting to coerce capitulation without a direct invasion. This is considered by many analysts as the most plausible Chinese military option. A blockade would be catastrophic for Taiwan's import-dependent economy (Taiwan imports most of its food and energy). But it would also invite international counter-pressure: US and allied navies would face a choice of backing down or forcing the blockade. A blockade that the US and allies do not break would signal that China has successfully challenged US dominance of the western Pacific — with enormous implications for every other country in the region. A Chinese blockade would be a slow-motion crisis — not a single dramatic event, but a grinding escalation with no clean decision point. It would force the US to choose between risking military confrontation to break the blockade, or accepting China's effective control of Taiwan's fate. Neither option is comfortable. This is precisely why blockade is attractive from China's strategic perspective.

  • 43 Scenario: A full military invasion +

    China launches an amphibious and airborne assault on Taiwan, attempting to achieve military control before the US can intervene effectively. This is the highest-risk scenario for all parties. An invasion would require crossing 180km of open water against prepared defenses — one of the most complex military operations imaginable. Most military analysts assess it as extremely difficult and costly. Taiwan has invested in weapons specifically designed to destroy amphibious landing forces. US military intervention would likely result in major losses for all sides. If it succeeded, it would transform the regional order. If it failed, it could destabilize China internally. Both outcomes would be catastrophic.

  • 44 Scenario: A negotiated settlement or political resolution +

    Both sides agree to some form of arrangement that addresses core concerns without military conflict. This might involve a formal declaration of no independence by Taiwan in exchange for reduced military pressure, or some innovative formula for coexistence. Currently the least likely scenario in the near term, given that Taiwan's electorate opposes any arrangement that compromises its de facto independence, and China has shown no willingness to offer the guarantees that would make reunification acceptable to Taiwan's population. But unexpected political changes in either Beijing or Taipei could create openings that don't currently exist.

  • 45 Scenario: Taiwan declares formal independence +

    Taiwan's government formally declares independence and seeks international recognition. This is what China has explicitly identified as a red line that would trigger military action. It is also what the US has repeatedly told Taiwan not to do — the US does not support Taiwanese independence and has made clear it would not back Taiwan if Taiwan unilaterally provoked a conflict through a formal independence declaration. Current Taiwanese governments have consistently avoided this — not because they don't value independence, but because provoking a military response that Taiwan and the US are not prepared for would be catastrophic. The status quo of de facto independence without formal declaration is safer than a formal declaration would be.

  • 46 Scenario: Long-term drift toward a new equilibrium +

    Over decades, Taiwan's international position gradually solidifies without formal declaration. More countries establish substantive unofficial ties. Taiwan joins more international organizations under various formulas. China's ability to diplomatically isolate Taiwan erodes. This is not independence — but it is not reunification either. It is a drift toward a permanent de facto separation that is increasingly recognized in practice if not in law. This scenario requires continued deterrence to prevent military disruption, continued economic ties to maintain Chinese restraint, and continued Taiwanese democratic legitimacy to sustain political will. This "long drift" scenario is arguably what has been happening for the past 40 years. Taiwan has become more internationally connected, more distinctly democratic, and more separately identified — without formal independence. Whether this can continue depends on whether the military and political pressures that could disrupt it remain manageable.

  • 47 What would a conflict actually look like for the rest of the world? +

    Not a distant regional war that others watch from afar. Semiconductor supply chains would collapse immediately — affecting every industry that uses electronics. Shipping through the Taiwan Strait would halt. Global financial markets would experience severe disruption. US-China trade — running at over $600 billion annually — would end. The US dollar's role as global reserve currency could be challenged. Countries would face immediate choices about which side to support. Energy prices would spike as the conflict risked spreading to the South China Sea. In economic terms, a Taiwan conflict would be the most disruptive event since World War II.

  • 48 What should you take away from all of this? +

    The China-Taiwan conflict is not a distant dispute between two governments over a small island. It is the central fault line of 21st-century geopolitics — where the world's two largest economies, competing visions of world order, the global semiconductor supply chain, and the future of democratic governance all intersect. It has lasted 75 years without war because each side has found the costs of changing the status quo too high. Those costs have not disappeared — but the balance of power is shifting, the identities of the people involved are diverging, and the strategic calculations of all parties are evolving. The one certainty is that how this conflict is managed — or mismanaged — will shape the world for the rest of this century. Final thought: Every major war of the 20th century was preceded by a period when informed observers concluded it was too costly to happen. The deterrence that has kept the Taiwan Strait peaceful is real — but it is not permanent. Paying attention to how it is maintained, and how it could fail, is not alarmism. It is basic strategic literacy.
