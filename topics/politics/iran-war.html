The US–Iran War:
What Is Actually Happening

On February 28, 2026, the United States and Israel launched surprise airstrikes on Iran.
They killed the Supreme Leader. They closed the Strait of Hormuz.
The world changed overnight.

This is not a story about bombs.
This is a story about why — and what comes next.

As of April 6, 2026: 5+ weeks of active warfare. 3,000+ killed. Oil at $100+/barrel. The Strait of Hormuz effectively closed. This is the most significant US military operation since 2003.

Part 1

What's Actually Happening

  • 01 Is the US actually at war with Iran? ↓

    Yes. Since February 28, 2026, the US and Israel have conducted coordinated military operations against Iran — codenamed Operation Epic Fury (US) and Operation Roaring Lion (Israel). This is not a proxy conflict or a limited strike. It is active, sustained warfare between two states. Despite the scale of the conflict, Congress never voted. Trump launched the war under executive authority. The last time the US formally declared war was 1942.

  • 02 When exactly did it begin? +

    February 28, 2026, at approximately 9:45 a.m. Iran Standard Time — during Ramadan morning prayers. The timing was deliberate: unusual for Israeli operations, it achieved tactical surprise. The first strikes hit Tehran and multiple Iranian cities simultaneously. Oman had reported a diplomatic "breakthrough" just 24 hours earlier. Iran's foreign minister was publicly talking about peace. The speed of the decision shocked even US allies.

  • 03 What triggered the strikes? +

    Three things converged: Iran's nuclear program had reached near-weapons capability (460kg of 60% enriched uranium — enough for approximately 11 bombs). The January 2026 massacre of protesters created political pressure to act. And Iran rejected a final offer — a civilian nuclear program with American investment in exchange for dismantling enrichment. Ask yourself: was the final offer genuine — or designed to be rejected, providing justification for a war that had already been decided?

  • 04 Was this a surprise attack? +

    Yes — operationally. Despite active negotiations, the US and Israel achieved complete tactical surprise through the early morning timing, simultaneous multi-front strikes, and a prior cyber operation that took Iran's internet offline for 60+ hours, dropping connectivity to 1%.

  • 05 Who is in charge in Iran now? +

    Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was assassinated in the opening strikes on February 28. Ali Larijani now heads an Interim Leadership Council. Khamenei's son Mojtaba has been formally selected as successor — a process that bypassed the normal clerical election and deepened internal divisions. Killing the Supreme Leader was the most significant targeted assassination in modern history. It either accelerates regime collapse or galvanizes resistance. Five weeks in, we don't yet know which.

  • 06 What is the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps? +

    Iran's elite ideological military force — separate from the regular army, loyal to the Supreme Leader. It controls Iran's missile and nuclear programs, runs its proxy networks across the Middle East, and owns roughly a third of Iran's economy. Thousands of IRGC personnel, including senior commanders, were killed in the opening strikes.

  • 07 Why do the US and Iran hate each other? +

    47 years of mutual grievance. The US backed the Shah's dictatorship and helped orchestrate the 1953 coup that overthrew Iran's elected government. Iran took 52 Americans hostage for 444 days in 1979, supports groups that have killed Americans across the region, and has pursued a nuclear program the US considers an existential threat. Ask yourself: which grievance came first? The answer depends entirely on which side of the timeline you start from.

  • 08 What happened in 1979 that changed everything? +

    The Islamic Revolution overthrew the US-backed Shah and replaced him with Ayatollah Khomeini's theocracy. Revolutionary students seized the US embassy and held 52 Americans hostage for 444 days. The US broke diplomatic relations and never restored them. Every conflict since flows from that rupture.

  • Part 2

    Why Now?

  • 09 Why did the US act in 2026 specifically? +

    Three factors aligned: Iran was at its weakest point in years — its economy in freefall, its proxies decimated by years of Israeli strikes, its population protesting. Its nuclear program had reached a threshold the US considered unacceptable. And Trump had returned to office with a mandate to act where Biden had hesitated. Wars rarely happen when one side is strong. They happen when one side calculates the other is weak enough that the cost will be acceptable. That calculation may yet prove wrong.

  • 10 What role did Israel play? +

    Equal partner, not subordinate. Israel conducted its largest combat sortie in history — over 1,200 bombs in the first 24 hours, targeting 500+ military sites. Netanyahu cited an "existential threat." Israel and the US coordinated closely, but Israeli officials emphasize they acted on independent decision-making, not American orders. This is not America's war with Israeli support. It is a joint war — with different primary objectives. The US wants to end Iran's nuclear program. Israel wants to end the regime.

  • 11 What does Iran's nuclear program have to do with this? +

    Everything. By late 2024, Iran had accumulated 460kg of 60% enriched uranium — enough fissile material for approximately 11 nuclear bombs on short notice. The war's stated primary objective is ensuring Iran "never possesses a nuclear weapon." Strikes have repeatedly targeted Natanz and other facilities.

  • 12 What was the "red line" that got crossed? +

    Iran's January 2026 massacre of thousands of protesters — described by some observers as "Iran's Babi Yar" — combined with Iran's refusal to accept "zero enrichment" in nuclear talks. Trump stated he was "not thrilled" with negotiations and acted unilaterally. Ask yourself: the humanitarian argument (protecting Iranian protesters) and the security argument (stopping nuclear weapons) point in the same direction. When two justifications align perfectly, it's worth asking which one is real.

  • 13 Did Congress approve this war? +

    No. Trump launched the operation under presidential authority as commander-in-chief. Congressional debate continues, with members challenging the legal basis for sustained operations. No formal declaration of war has been issued.

  • 14 What role does Trump play? +

    Central architect and sole commander. Trump ordered the strikes from Air Force One. He defines all war objectives personally and manages the conflict through social media announcements and national addresses. His stated goals have shifted multiple times — from "regime change encouragement" to "unconditional surrender" to hints of negotiations — sometimes within 24 hours. Parsing Trump's actual strategy from his rhetoric on this war is genuinely difficult. That ambiguity may be intentional — or it may be the absence of a clear strategy.

  • 15 Has the US fought Iran before? +

    Not directly at this scale. The 1980s Tanker War involved naval clashes. The US shot down an Iranian civilian airliner in 1988, killing 290 people. Proxy conflicts have raged for decades — in Iraq, Syria, Yemen. But this is the first direct, sustained warfare between the two countries.

  • 16 What sparked the final escalation before the war? +

    The June 2025 Twelve-Day War — previous US-Israeli strikes on Iran — established the military precedent and degraded Iran's defenses. When protests erupted in December 2025 and were brutally suppressed in January 2026, the Trump administration concluded Iran's regime was vulnerable and its nuclear timeline unacceptable.

  • Part 3

    The War Itself

  • 17 What has the US actually bombed? +

    Over 8,000 Iranian military targets — nuclear facilities (Natanz repeatedly), missile production sites, 130+ naval vessels destroyed, IRGC bases, government buildings including parliament, and leadership compounds. Civilian infrastructure including hospitals, schools, and bridges has also been struck. The expansion from military to civilian infrastructure is legally significant. Trump has explicitly threatened to hit power and water desalination plants — which international law experts have called a potential war crime.

  • 18 How many strikes have taken place? +

    The opening 24 hours saw 1,200+ Israeli bombs alone. By late March, CENTCOM reported 8,000+ targets struck. Over 3,000 distinct attack events have been documented across 29 of Iran's 31 provinces. The pace continues daily with waves of Tomahawk missiles, fighter jets, and bunker-busters. At least 2,076 Iranians have been killed and 26,500 wounded according to Iran's Health Ministry.

  • 19 How strong is Iran's military? +

    Degraded but not destroyed. The US claims Iran's navy is "annihilated," its air force grounded, its missile defenses eliminated. But Iran continues launching drones and ballistic missiles, has shot down two US fighter jets, and maintains the Strait of Hormuz blockade. Iran cannot win on the battlefield — its strategy is to make the war painful enough that the US concludes victory is too costly.

  • 20 What weapons is Iran using? +

    Shahed drones, ballistic missiles (potentially including ICBMs), and anti-ship missiles. Iran has struck US bases across the Gulf, Dubai's port and airport, Kuwait's power and desalination plants, and oil facilities in Fujairah. It has also deployed cyberattacks and proxy forces across the region.

  • 21 Has Iran retaliated — and how effectively? +

    Massively. Hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles fired at Israel and US bases in Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE. A drone struck Britain's Akrotiri base in Cyprus. Missiles were reportedly launched at Diego Garcia. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed, stranding 20,000 sailors and thousands of ships. Iran cannot win this war militarily. But it can make it economically intolerable for the world — and politically untenable for Trump. The Strait closure is its most powerful weapon.

  • 22 What are Iran's proxies? +

    Armed groups funded, trained, and directed by Iran across the region: Hezbollah in Lebanon, Houthis in Yemen, Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad in Gaza, and Iraqi Shiite militias. These groups extend Iran's reach without direct attribution — and have been significantly weakened by years of Israeli strikes before this war began.

  • 23 What is Hezbollah's role? +

    Full combatant. Hezbollah restarted hostilities against Israel on March 2, 2026, firing 1,800+ rockets and drones. Israel invaded southern Lebanon in response. By early April, the 2026 Lebanon war had killed 1,400+ people and displaced nearly one million civilians.

  • 24 What is happening in Lebanon right now? +

    Active war. Israeli ground forces entered southern Lebanon on March 16, 2026. Daily airstrikes target Beirut and the Bekaa Valley. The Lebanese government has banned Hezbollah's military activities and expelled Iran's ambassador — but fighting continues with no ceasefire in sight. Ask yourself: how many simultaneous wars can one region sustain? Iran, Lebanon, Gaza, Yemen — the Middle East is experiencing its most intense period of conflict since at least the 1980s.

  • Part 4

    The Turning Points

  • 25 What happened with the downed US fighter jet? +

    On April 3, 2026 — day 35 of the war — Iran shot down a US F-15E Strike Eagle over central Iran. Both crew ejected. One was rescued on April 5 by US special forces deep inside Iranian territory — requiring "dozens of aircraft" and Israeli intelligence cooperation. The second crew member's status remains unknown, with Iran offering rewards for capture. Two downed US jets in one day — after officials had claimed "total air dominance" — shattered that narrative. It is the most visible evidence that Iran's military is not as destroyed as claimed.

  • 26 Why was that incident so important? +

    Because it proved Iran can still hurt the US. An F-15E is one of the world's most advanced fighter jets. Iran shot one down over its own territory — meaning its air defense, despite weeks of bombardment, remains functional. The rescue operation demonstrated that US personnel are operating in extremely high-risk environments, not a "clean" air campaign.

  • 27 Could Iran close the Strait of Hormuz? +

    It already has. Since early March 2026, shipping through the Strait — which carries 20% of global oil and gas — has slowed to a standstill. 150+ freight ships are stalled. Insurance costs became unsustainable. Iran has mined approaches and attacked tankers. Trump set a 48-hour ultimatum to reopen it. Iran called the ultimatum "helpless, nervous, unbalanced and stupid."

  • 28 What happens if it stays closed? +

    Global economic catastrophe. Federal Reserve models predict oil at $98–$132/barrel and global GDP growth falling 0.2–1.3 percentage points depending on duration. Asia, which receives 80% of Gulf oil exports, faces severe supply disruptions. Iraq — which exports most of its oil through the Strait — is already in economic crisis. Ask yourself: Iran's economy is already destroyed by sanctions. The Strait closure hurts everyone else more than it hurts Iran. That is its leverage.

  • 29 Why are oil prices rising globally? +

    Oil is already above $100/barrel. The Strait closure removes roughly 20% of world oil supply. Iranian strikes have hit Gulf refineries — satellite imagery confirms three fuel tanks destroyed in Fujairah, fires at Dubai's port. Airlines are hiking fares due to jet fuel shortages. The economic damage compounds daily.

  • 30 Has cyberwarfare been used? +

    Yes — from the very beginning. The opening strikes included cyberattacks that caused a 60+ hour internet blackout across Iran, dropping connectivity to 1%. Messages were sent urging Iranians to rise against their government. Iran has since restricted internet access to government-approved users only. The information war is as real as the air war. Cutting a country's internet during a military strike is a new kind of warfare — and its legality under international law is entirely untested.

  • 31 Is this already a regional war? +

    Yes — unambiguously. Direct combat spans Iran, Israel, and Lebanon. Iranian strikes have hit Saudi Arabia, UAE, Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Jordan, Iraq, and Cyprus. Syria and Yemen remain active secondary fronts. Over 10 nations have been attacked. The regional order established since 1979 is visibly collapsing.

  • Part 5

    Global Impact

  • 32 Where do Russia and China stand? +

    Both oppose the war diplomatically. Russia condemned strikes on Natanz as a "blatant violation of international law" and evacuated 200 staff from Iran's Bushehr nuclear plant after it was hit. Putin and Erdogan jointly called for an immediate ceasefire. China has called for restraint. Neither has intervened militarily. Iran expected more support from Russia and China. Their restraint leaves Iran genuinely isolated — and may ultimately force it to negotiate.

  • 33 What do US allies think? +

    Divided. The UK has deployed RAF defensively after its Cyprus base was struck by an Iranian drone. France's Macron called Trump's threat to use force to reopen the Strait "unrealistic." Trump called NATO a "paper tiger" for not backing the US. Gulf states that sought détente with Tehran are now military targets.

  • 34 What does this mean for Israel's security? +

    Paradoxical. Israel achieved major objectives — Khamenei is dead, Iran's military is degraded, Hezbollah weakened. But Israel faces daily missile barrages from Iran and Hezbollah. Nearly 7,000 Israelis have been injured. The Lebanon invasion creates new long-term security commitments with no clear endpoint.

  • 35 What does this mean for Saudi Arabia? +

    Saudi Arabia lobbied Trump to strike Iran and hosts US bases used in the campaign. In return, it has become a direct target — Iranian strikes have hit Aramco refineries and the Eastern Province. Saudi Arabia had been seeking to reduce tensions with Iran; now it is a combat zone. Its bet that the US would finish this quickly may not pay out. Ask yourself: if this war drags on for years, will Saudi Arabia look back on its role as a strategic triumph — or a catastrophic miscalculation?

  • 36 Could this destabilize the entire Middle East? +

    It already has. One million displaced in Lebanon. Protests in Pakistan and Iraq. Syria and Yemen as active secondary fronts. The regional order that has — however imperfectly — structured the Middle East since 1979 is collapsing. What comes after is genuinely unknown.

  • 37 What's happening inside Iran? +

    Difficult to verify — Iran has imposed martial law and restricted internet to government-approved users. There are both pro-government rallies mourning Khamenei and anti-regime sentiment fueled by economic collapse (the rial has lost 90% of its value since 2018). The Tehran Prosecutor's Office has seized assets of 100+ individuals accused of "supporting the enemy." Bombing a population to trigger regime change has a poor historical record. Iranians who hate their government may hate foreign bombs even more. National identity can override political grievance.

  • 38 Are there protests or internal unrest? +

    The January 2026 protests were crushed with thousands killed. Current conditions — internet blackout, martial law — make organized protest essentially impossible. Post-war, Iran faces potential fragmentation: Kurdish, Baloch, and Azeri minorities may seek autonomy if central authority collapses.

  • 39 How is the global economy affected? +

    Severely. Oil above $100/barrel. Federal Reserve models project a potential 2.9% annualized GDP drop in Q2 2026 alone. European energy crisis deepening with LNG shortages. Supply chains disrupted globally. The war is adding major inflationary pressure simultaneously with Trump's tariff war — two economic shocks at once.

  • Part 6

    The Big Questions

  • 40 Can Iran still build a nuclear weapon? +

    Unknown. Natanz has been repeatedly struck with bunker-busters. The US claims Iran's program is "obliterated." But underground facilities may survive. Without IAEA inspectors — who have urged restraint — certainty is impossible. Iran had 460kg of near-weapons-grade uranium before the war. Where that material is now is a critical unanswered question.

  • 41 What would a nuclear Iran change? +

    Everything. A nuclear-armed Iran would be effectively uninvadable. It would provide a nuclear umbrella for its proxies. It would trigger Saudi Arabia and Turkey to seek their own nuclear programs. It would fundamentally alter the regional balance of power for generations. Ask yourself: if this war fails to end Iran's nuclear ambitions — and instead accelerates them underground — was it worth the cost?

  • 42 Could this war end quickly? +

    Trump claims it will be "decisive, not endless." But after 5+ weeks, Iran continues fighting, Hormuz remains closed, and the regime has not collapsed. Former Iranian Foreign Minister Zarif has proposed peace terms. Indirect talks are ongoing. Trump has extended deadlines. The "quick victory" assumption mirrors Iraq 2003 — and we know how that ended.

  • 43 What would a ground invasion look like? +

    Catastrophic. A ground invasion would require 100,000+ troops, face mountainous terrain, and encounter fierce resistance. Iran is three times the size of Iraq with four times the population. Trump has not ruled it out. Iran's military has explicitly warned it would be the beginning of a quagmire that makes Afghanistan look manageable. The US learned in Iraq and Afghanistan that winning from the air is possible. Winning on the ground in a country that doesn't want you is something else entirely.

  • 44 Is there an exit strategy? +

    Not publicly defined. Trump has stated six demands Iran must meet but has shifted the goalposts repeatedly. He extended an April 7 deadline for hitting Iranian power plants. Pakistan is attempting to broker talks. History suggests that wars without clear exit strategies tend to last longer and cost more than anyone planned.

  • 45 Could this trigger World War III? +

    Low probability — high consequence. Direct US-Russia or US-China conflict remains unlikely. But escalation through proxies, cyber, or accidental strikes — like the drone that hit Britain's Cyprus base — could spiral. The regional scope of this war already exceeds most post-1945 conflicts.

  • 46 Who is actually winning right now? +

    Tactically, the US and Israel have destroyed most of Iran's conventional military capabilities. Strategically, the picture is different. Iran retains missile and drone capacity, the Strait closure leverage, and proxy forces. The regime survives. The popular uprising hasn't materialized. The global economy suffers. Military dominance and strategic victory are not the same thing. The US has the former. Whether it achieves the latter depends on how this ends — not how it started.

  • 47 Is regime change the real goal? +

    Trump denies it — then says it "will happen." US officials speak of "neutering" Iran "for a very, very long time." The assassination of Khamenei and systematic targeting of Iran's leadership suggest decapitation is policy, even if occupation is not. Israel has been more explicit: it wants the Islamic Republic gone.

  • 48 Is this war about oil or power? +

    Both — inseparably. Oil is power. Controlling Gulf energy flows underwrites US global dominance and protects allies. But it's also about nuclear proliferation, Israeli security, domestic politics, and Trump's "America First" projection. This war has multiple overlapping rationales — which makes it harder to end, because "victory" means different things to different participants.

  • 49 Why is this war different from Iraq or Afghanistan? +

    Four things: Speed — massive strikes in hours, not months. Technology — unprecedented cyber, drone, and precision munitions. Objectives — destroy capabilities, not occupy territory (so far). And risk — Iran can actually strike back, creating mutual vulnerability that was absent in Iraq and Afghanistan. Iraq fell in three weeks. Iran will not. Whatever comes next, it will not resemble the opening phases of either previous war.

  • 50 What do most people misunderstand about this war? +

    That it is "surgical" or "limited." This is comprehensive warfare targeting a nation's leadership, military, economy, and infrastructure. The civilian toll is mounting. The "no boots on the ground" narrative ignores special forces operations deep inside Iran. And the stated objectives keep shifting — which is usually a sign that no one is certain what winning actually looks like. The most dangerous thing about this war is not the bombs. It is the certainty — on all sides — that they are completely right.

  • 51 What happens next? +

    Three possible paths. Negotiations succeed — Iran makes major concessions on its nuclear program and the war ends. The regime collapses — a new government emerges and a deal is reached. Or the war grinds on — an air campaign that damages Iran severely but achieves no decisive political outcome, becoming the most expensive stalemate since Vietnam. Ask yourself: which of these three outcomes do you think is most likely — and why? The answer tells you more about your assumptions than about the war.

  • 52 What should you take away from all of this? +

    Wars are easier to start than to finish. The US entered this conflict with clear objectives and overwhelming military superiority. Five weeks in, the Strait is still closed, the regime is still standing, and two US jets have been shot down. History does not grade wars on how they begin. It grades them on how they end. Final thought: Every war in history was started by people who believed it would be shorter than it was.
