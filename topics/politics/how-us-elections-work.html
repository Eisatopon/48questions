How US Elections
Actually Work

You've watched the results come in. You've heard "Electoral College," "swing state," "midterms."

But do you actually understand why the world's oldest democracy elects its president this way — and whether it was always like this?

48 questions. No spin. No party line. Just the system — and what it means.

Before you start: The US doesn't have one election system. It has fifty state systems, one federal system, and centuries of accumulated rules, amendments, and court decisions. This guide cuts through all of it.

Part 1

The Basics

  • 01 How is the US government structured? +

    Three branches: the Executive (the President), the Legislative (Congress — which includes the Senate and the House of Representatives), and the Judicial (the Supreme Court and lower federal courts). Each branch has powers that check the other two. No branch is supposed to dominate. This separation of powers was designed by the Founders specifically to prevent tyranny — which they had just fought a revolution against. Every friction in American politics today is, in part, that design working as intended.

  • 02 Who gets to vote in the US? +

    Today: any US citizen aged 18 or over who registers to vote. But that sentence took nearly 200 years to become true. Originally only white male property owners could vote. African Americans gained the constitutional right in 1870 — but were systematically blocked until the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Women gained the right to vote in 1920. The voting age was lowered from 21 to 18 in 1971. Ask yourself: the US is the world's oldest constitutional democracy. It had universal suffrage for less than 60 years. Everything before that was something else.

  • 03 Why does the US have only two major parties? +

    Because of the winner-takes-all (first-past-the-post) electoral system. In most US elections, only one candidate wins — the one with the most votes, even if that's 35%. Third parties get millions of votes and zero seats. This creates a structural incentive for voters to choose the "lesser evil" between the two big parties rather than "waste" a vote on a third party. Most democracies use proportional representation — where a party that gets 15% of the vote gets roughly 15% of the seats. The US does not. That single design choice explains most of American political polarization.

  • 04 What is a primary election? +

    An election within a party to decide who will be that party's candidate in the general election. Before anyone votes for President, Senate, or House in November, each party holds primaries where registered party members choose their nominee. Primary voters tend to be more partisan than the general electorate — which pulls candidates toward the extremes before they pivot to the center for the general election. The US is one of very few countries that uses public elections to choose party nominees. In most countries, parties choose candidates internally. Primaries give ordinary voters more power — but also produce more extreme candidates.

  • 05 What is a caucus? +

    An older form of party nominating process where voters physically gather in rooms and publicly express support for candidates — sometimes moving to different corners, sometimes in open discussion. Iowa famously uses caucuses for presidential nominations. They are less secret, more time-consuming, and tend to favor passionate activists over casual voters. Most states have switched to primaries.

  • 06 Who runs elections in the US? +

    The states — not the federal government. Each of the 50 states sets its own rules for voter registration, polling hours, ID requirements, mail voting, and ballot design. This is why election rules vary dramatically: some states allow same-day registration; others require registration weeks in advance. Some states mail ballots to every voter automatically; others require in-person voting. There is no single "US election system." There are 50 different state systems, each run by state officials who are often themselves elected — and who may belong to one of the two parties competing in the very elections they administer.

  • 07 What is gerrymandering? +

    The manipulation of electoral district boundaries by the party in power to give itself an advantage. After each census (every 10 years), states redraw their congressional districts. If one party controls the state legislature, it can draw districts that pack opposition voters into a few seats, or spread them inefficiently across many — resulting in more seats than the party's vote share would warrant. The word comes from Governor Elbridge Gerry of Massachusetts, who in 1812 approved a district shaped like a salamander to benefit his party. "Gerry" + "salamander" = "gerrymander." More than 200 years later, the practice is more sophisticated and just as common.

  • 08 What is campaign finance — and why does it matter? +

    US elections are extraordinarily expensive. The 2024 presidential election cost an estimated $16 billion — the most expensive in history. Candidates spend this on TV ads, ground operations, digital advertising, and staff. After the Supreme Court's Citizens United decision (2010), corporations and unions can spend unlimited amounts on political advertising through "super PACs" — political committees that can accept unlimited donations. Ask yourself: if corporations can spend unlimited money influencing elections, and elections determine who makes laws that affect corporations — is that a democracy or something else?

  • Part 2

    Electing the President

  • 09 Do Americans directly elect the President? +

    No. Americans vote for a slate of "electors" who then formally elect the President. When you cast a vote for a presidential candidate, you are technically voting for electors pledged to that candidate. The electors meet in December in their state capitals and cast the actual votes for President. The winner of the national popular vote does not automatically become President. Most Americans don't fully grasp this. They believe they are voting directly for President. They are not. They are voting in 50 separate state elections, each of which produces electoral votes, not a direct national tally.

  • 10 What is the Electoral College? +

    A body of 538 electors — one for each of the 535 members of Congress (100 senators + 435 representatives) plus 3 for Washington DC. To win the presidency, a candidate must receive at least 270 electoral votes — a majority. Each state gets a number of electors equal to its total congressional delegation.

  • 11 How are electoral votes distributed among states? +

    California has 54 electoral votes (the most). Wyoming has 3 (the minimum — every state gets at least 3). The distribution changes after each census as population shifts. Populous states have more electoral votes, but small states are overrepresented relative to their population: Wyoming voters have roughly four times the electoral influence per capita of California voters. This imbalance is not a bug — it was a feature, designed to protect smaller states. Whether that protection is still necessary or appropriate 235 years later is one of the most contested debates in American politics.

  • 12 What is winner-takes-all and how does it work in presidential elections? +

    In 48 of 50 states, the presidential candidate who wins the most popular votes in that state receives all of that state's electoral votes — regardless of the margin. Win California by 1 vote and you get all 54 electoral votes. Lose Texas by 1 vote and you get zero of its 40 electoral votes. Only Maine and Nebraska allocate some electoral votes by congressional district. This is why a candidate can win the national popular vote and still lose the presidency — it has happened five times. The game isn't won by total votes; it's won by winning the right states.

  • 13 Can a candidate win the presidency without winning the popular vote? +

    Yes — and it has happened five times: 1824 (John Quincy Adams), 1876 (Rutherford B. Hayes), 1888 (Benjamin Harrison), 2000 (George W. Bush over Al Gore), and 2016 (Donald Trump over Hillary Clinton, who won the popular vote by nearly 3 million votes). In each case, the winner had fewer total votes nationwide but won the right combination of states. In 2016, Trump won by flipping three states — Pennsylvania, Michigan, and Wisconsin — by a combined margin of about 77,000 votes. Those 77,000 votes, spread across three states, determined the presidency despite a 3 million vote national deficit.

  • 14 What is a swing state — and why does it dominate presidential campaigns? +

    A state where neither party reliably wins — where the outcome is genuinely uncertain. Because California will almost certainly vote Democratic and Texas Republican, candidates spend almost no time or money there. Instead, campaigns focus everything on 7-8 states that could go either way: Pennsylvania, Michigan, Wisconsin, Arizona, Georgia, Nevada, North Carolina, and sometimes others. Ask yourself: in a country of 330 million people, presidential elections are effectively decided by a few hundred thousand voters in a handful of states. Is that a national election — or a series of regional ones?

  • 15 What happens if no candidate reaches 270 electoral votes? +

    The House of Representatives chooses the President — but with a twist: each state delegation gets one vote, not each representative. So Wyoming (1 representative) has equal weight to California (52 representatives). The Senate separately chooses the Vice President. This has happened twice: 1800 and 1824. In a close election with a strong third-party candidate, it remains a real possibility. If this happened today, it would likely mean the House — dominated by whichever party controls more state delegations — would choose a president who lost the popular vote. The political crisis that would follow is difficult to imagine.

  • 16 Why was the Electoral College created? +

    Several reasons, all rooted in 1787. The Founders distrusted direct democracy — they feared an uninformed public could be manipulated into choosing a demagogue. They also needed to satisfy smaller states (who feared being dominated by large ones) and Southern slave states (whose enslaved population couldn't vote but counted toward representation via the Three-Fifths Compromise). The Electoral College was the compromise that made all parties accept the Constitution. Two of the three original reasons no longer apply: slavery was abolished, and information is no longer scarce. The third — protecting small states — is the one most defenders still cite today.

  • 17 What is the presidential term — and can a president serve more than two terms? +

    Four years. The 22nd Amendment (ratified 1951) limits presidents to two elected terms — a maximum of 10 years if they served part of another president's term. Before 1951 there was no legal limit. Franklin D. Roosevelt won four presidential elections (1932, 1936, 1940, 1944) and served until his death in 1945. The two-term limit was passed directly in response to FDR's unprecedented tenure.

  • 18 What are the requirements to become President? +

    Only three constitutional requirements: natural-born US citizen, at least 35 years old, and a US resident for at least 14 years. That's it. No educational requirement. No experience requirement. No criminal background check. The Constitution leaves it entirely to voters to assess fitness for the most powerful office in the world. Ask yourself: you need more qualifications to become a licensed plumber than to become President of the United States. The Founders trusted voters to filter for competence. Whether that trust is well-placed is an ongoing experiment.

  • Part 3

    The Senate

  • 19 What is the Senate? +

    The upper chamber of Congress. It has 100 members — two senators from each of the 50 states. The Senate was designed to be the "cooling" chamber — slower, more deliberate, less responsive to immediate public passions than the House. Senators serve 6-year terms and must be at least 30 years old. The Founders compared the Senate to a saucer — used to "cool" legislation that came from the House "too hot." Today the Senate is often where legislation goes to die — its rules allow a minority of senators to block almost anything indefinitely.

  • 20 Why does every state get two senators regardless of population? +

    This was the price of the Constitutional Convention. Large states (Virginia, New York) wanted representation proportional to population. Small states (Delaware, New Jersey) refused to ratify a constitution that would give large states permanent dominance. The "Great Compromise" of 1787 created a bicameral legislature: the House would represent population, the Senate would give every state equal standing. The result: Wyoming (580,000 people) has the same Senate power as California (39 million). The 26 smallest states — representing about 17% of the US population — can elect a Senate majority. This is the most significant structural imbalance in American democracy.

  • 21 What exclusive powers does the Senate have? +

    Several major ones. The Senate must confirm presidential nominees — cabinet members, federal judges, Supreme Court justices, ambassadors. The Senate must ratify international treaties by a two-thirds majority. The Senate holds impeachment trials when the House impeaches the President or other officials. And the Senate confirms or rejects nominees to thousands of federal positions. The power to confirm Supreme Court justices may be the Senate's most consequential function. Justices serve for life, and a single confirmation can shape American law for 30-40 years.

  • 22 What is the filibuster? +

    A Senate procedure that allows any senator to extend debate on a bill indefinitely — effectively blocking a vote. To end a filibuster (invoke "cloture"), 60 of 100 senators must agree. Since most major legislation needs 60 votes to advance — not just 51 — the minority party can block almost any bill it opposes. The filibuster is not in the Constitution; it's a Senate rule that evolved over time. The filibuster was used for decades by Southern senators to block civil rights legislation. Today it is used routinely by whichever party is in the minority. It effectively means 41 senators — potentially representing a small fraction of the population — can block any legislation indefinitely.

  • 23 How are senators elected? +

    By statewide popular vote — but this was not always the case. Until the 17th Amendment (1913), senators were chosen by state legislatures, not by voters directly. The amendment was passed after years of corruption scandals in which wealthy interests bought Senate seats through state legislatures. Direct election was meant to make senators more accountable to the public. Ask yourself: it took 124 years after the Constitution was ratified before ordinary Americans could vote directly for their senators. The Senate was designed to be insulated from the public — and for over a century, it was.

  • 24 What is a Senate class — and why are only some senators up for election at once? +

    Senators are divided into three "classes" — roughly one third of seats up for election every two years. Class I senators face election in presidential years. Class II and III face election in midterms. This staggered structure means the Senate never fully turns over at once — providing continuity and making the Senate more resistant to sudden shifts in public opinion than the House, where every seat is contested every two years.

  • 25 What happens when a Senate seat becomes vacant between elections? +

    The governor of the state typically appoints a temporary senator to serve until a special election can be held — though the exact rules vary by state. Some states require an immediate special election; others allow the governor's appointment to serve until the next regular election. This means a single governor can dramatically change the Senate balance of power with one appointment.

  • 26 Who is the most powerful person in the Senate? +

    The Senate Majority Leader — the elected leader of whichever party holds more than 50 seats. The Majority Leader controls the Senate's legislative calendar: which bills come to the floor, when votes are held, and which nominees move forward. This position has no equivalent in the Constitution — it is a party role that evolved into the most powerful office in the chamber. Mitch McConnell (Republican Majority Leader 2015-2021) blocked a Supreme Court nomination for nearly a year by simply refusing to schedule a vote. No law allowed this. No law prevented it. Senate rules are what the majority decides they are.

  • Part 4

    The House of Representatives

  • 27 What is the House of Representatives? +

    The lower chamber of Congress — the chamber designed to be most directly responsive to the public. It has 435 members, each representing a congressional district of roughly 760,000 people. Representatives serve two-year terms and must be at least 25 years old. The entire House is up for election every two years — both in presidential years and in midterms. The House was designed to be the "people's chamber" — close to voters, quick to respond to shifts in public opinion. The Senate was designed to slow it down. Together they were supposed to balance popular responsiveness with stability.

  • 28 Why are there exactly 435 House seats? +

    It's arbitrary. The original House had 65 members. As the population grew, Congress added seats after each census. In 1929, Congress passed the Permanent Apportionment Act, capping the House at 435 — the number it had at the time. No constitutional reason. The cap was a political compromise. Today the US has the lowest ratio of representatives to citizens of any large democracy. A larger House would give more representation to more people. But a larger House also means less power for each individual member. The 435 who benefit from the cap are exactly the 435 who would have to vote to change it.

  • 29 What exclusive powers does the House have? +

    All revenue bills (tax legislation) must originate in the House — this reflects the principle that the people's closest representatives should control taxation. The House also has the sole power of impeachment — the power to charge the President or other officials with "high crimes and misdemeanors." If the House votes to impeach, the Senate then holds the trial.

  • 30 How is gerrymandering done — and can it be stopped? +

    After each census, state legislatures redraw district boundaries. A party in control of the legislature can draw maps that pack opposition voters into a few districts (where they win by huge margins and "waste" votes) or crack them across many districts (where they always lose narrowly). The result: a party can win 60% of seats with 50% of the vote. The Supreme Court has ruled that federal courts cannot strike down partisan gerrymandering — leaving it to states. About a dozen states have moved to independent redistricting commissions — nonpartisan bodies that draw maps without legislative interference. The evidence suggests these produce fairer maps. Most states have not adopted them.

  • 31 Who is the Speaker of the House? +

    The leader of the majority party in the House — elected by all House members at the start of each Congress. The Speaker controls the House floor: which bills are debated, when votes are held, who speaks. The Speaker is also second in the line of presidential succession after the Vice President — meaning if both the President and Vice President die or are removed, the Speaker becomes President.

  • 32 What is apportionment — and how does it work? +

    After each census (held every 10 years), the 435 House seats are redistributed among states based on population changes. Fast-growing states gain seats; shrinking states lose them. Texas gained two seats after the 2020 census; California lost one for the first time in its history. Apportionment affects both House representation and the number of electoral votes each state has in presidential elections.

  • 33 Can one party control the House and the other control the Senate? +

    Yes — and it happens regularly. This "split Congress" makes passing legislation nearly impossible, since both chambers must agree on identical bill text before it goes to the President. It also produces divided government if the President belongs to the party that controls neither chamber. The Founders built this friction deliberately — they believed gridlock was safer than unchecked power. Ask yourself: a system designed so that nothing happens unless multiple institutions agree — is that a feature or a bug? It depends entirely on what you want the government to do.

  • 34 What are congressional committees — and why do they matter? +

    Both chambers divide their work into committees — specialized groups that review, amend, and vote on legislation before it reaches the full chamber. The Appropriations Committee controls spending. The Judiciary Committee handles judicial nominations and legal matters. The Ways and Means Committee handles tax legislation. Virtually no bill reaches the floor without first passing through committee — giving committee chairs enormous power to advance or kill legislation.

  • Part 5

    Midterm Elections

  • 35 What are midterm elections? +

    Elections held two years into a president's four-year term — exactly halfway between presidential elections. All 435 House seats are contested, along with roughly one third of Senate seats (whichever class is up that cycle), most governorships, and thousands of state and local races. There is no presidential race on the ballot.

  • 36 Why do midterms matter so much? +

    Because they determine who controls Congress — and Congress controls legislation, spending, and oversight of the executive. A president who loses one or both chambers in the midterms can see their entire agenda blocked. Bill Clinton, Barack Obama, Donald Trump, and Joe Biden all lost the House in their first midterm election. Midterms effectively function as a national referendum on the sitting president. A midterm election that flips the House changes more than who sits in Congress. It changes what laws can pass, what investigations can be launched, and what nominees can be confirmed. The president is the same — but the government is different.

  • 37 Why does the president's party almost always lose seats in midterms? +

    A consistent historical pattern with multiple explanations. Voters who are satisfied with the status quo tend to stay home; dissatisfied voters are more motivated to turn out. The president's party often overperforms in the preceding presidential election, leaving fewer persuadable seats to defend. And two years is enough time for economic disappointments or policy controversies to accumulate. In 19 of the last 22 midterms, the president's party lost House seats.

  • 38 What is voter turnout — and why is it lower in midterms? +

    Voter turnout measures what percentage of eligible voters actually vote. In recent presidential elections, turnout has been around 55-65%. In midterms, it typically falls to 40-50% — and sometimes lower. Without the presidential race to drive attention, casual voters are less engaged. The voters who do show up in midterms tend to be older, more partisan, and more ideologically extreme than the general electorate. This matters enormously: midterm electorates are systematically different from presidential electorates. Candidates who win midterms often hold positions more extreme than what a full-turnout electorate would support. Democracy's quality varies with turnout.

  • 39 What happens at the state level in midterms? +

    Most governorships and many state legislative seats are also contested in midterms. This matters beyond state policy: governors control how elections are administered, and state legislatures draw congressional district boundaries after each census. Winning state-level races in the midterm before a census year can determine which party controls redistricting for the entire following decade. The 2010 midterms — a Republican wave — gave Republicans control of enough state legislatures to dominate redistricting after the 2010 census. Democrats spent the following decade competing in maps drawn to disadvantage them. State races have federal consequences.

  • 40 What is a "wave election"? +

    An election in which one party wins dramatically more seats than normal — sweeping across the country rather than winning on a district-by-district basis. Wave elections often reflect a national mood: economic crisis, presidential unpopularity, or a galvanizing issue. The 1994 Republican Revolution, the 2006 Democratic sweep, the 2010 Tea Party wave, and the 2018 Democratic House takeover are all examples. Waves can flip chambers that seemed safe.

  • 41 What is lame duck — and when does it apply? +

    A "lame duck" is an elected official who is still in office but will not continue after the end of their term — either because they lost an election, are term-limited, or chose not to run again. The lame duck period between the November election and the January swearing-in (for Congress) or January 20th (for the President) is when outgoing officials still hold power but have diminished political influence. Presidents sometimes use this period to pardon offenders, issue executive orders, or confirm judges.

  • 42 How has the midterm map changed in recent decades? +

    The geographic sorting of American politics has made midterms increasingly predictable at the district level — and increasingly contested at the national level. Urban areas have become overwhelmingly Democratic; rural areas overwhelmingly Republican. The number of genuinely competitive districts has shrunk dramatically. As a result, national midterm outcomes increasingly hinge on a small number of suburban swing districts in a handful of states. A country of 330 million people, deeply divided on almost every major issue, now sees its national direction determined by a few hundred thousand voters in suburban Philadelphia, suburban Detroit, and suburban Atlanta. That's the current reality of American democracy.

  • Part 6

    History & Change

  • 43 How has the electoral system changed since 1787? +

    Profoundly — mostly through constitutional amendments. The 12th Amendment (1804) fixed a flaw that caused Jefferson and his running mate to tie in electoral votes. The 15th Amendment (1870) gave Black men the right to vote. The 17th Amendment (1913) established direct election of senators. The 19th Amendment (1920) gave women the right to vote. The 24th Amendment (1964) abolished poll taxes. The 26th Amendment (1971) lowered the voting age to 18. Each amendment expanded who could participate in democracy. The story of American electoral history is a slow, contested, often violent expansion of who counts as a full citizen. Each expansion required a political fight — and each was resisted by those who benefited from the status quo.

  • 44 What was the original Electoral College supposed to look like? +

    The Founders imagined electors as independent wise men who would exercise their own judgment — not as rubber stamps for whoever won the popular vote in their state. They did not anticipate political parties, winner-takes-all state laws, or the concept of "faithless electors." In the first election (1788), there was essentially no campaign — George Washington was simply chosen by consensus. The party system that took over within one decade was entirely unplanned. Almost everything distinctive about how US elections work today — political parties, primaries, winner-takes-all states, campaign finance — was not designed. It evolved. The system the Founders created and the system that actually operates bear only a partial resemblance to each other.

  • 45 Could the US ever abolish the Electoral College? +

    Only through a constitutional amendment — which requires two-thirds of both chambers of Congress and ratification by three-quarters of states (38 of 50). Small states, which benefit from the Electoral College's overrepresentation of small states, would never ratify such an amendment. An alternative being pursued by some states: the National Popular Vote Interstate Compact, where states agree to give their electoral votes to whoever wins the national popular vote — a workaround that doesn't require a constitutional amendment. Ask yourself: the states most overrepresented by the Electoral College are the ones that would have to vote to abolish it. That is the reason it will almost certainly survive — not because a majority of Americans support it, but because the system protects those who benefit from it.

  • 46 What is the Voting Rights Act — and what happened to it? +

    The landmark 1965 legislation that banned discriminatory voting practices and required states with histories of discrimination to get federal approval ("preclearance") before changing their voting laws. It was the most effective piece of civil rights legislation in American history. In 2013, the Supreme Court's Shelby County v. Holder decision effectively gutted the preclearance requirement, ruling that the formula for determining which states needed oversight was outdated. Within hours of the ruling, several states began implementing voting restrictions they had previously been blocked from enacting. The Voting Rights Act addressed a specific, documented pattern of racial voter suppression. When the Supreme Court removed its enforcement mechanism, that suppression — in new forms — returned rapidly. The connection between election law and racial power was not theoretical.

  • 47 What do most people misunderstand about US elections? +

    That there is one election. There isn't. There are 50 different state elections for the presidency, hundreds of separate congressional races, thousands of state and local races, primaries before primaries, caucuses, special elections, runoffs, and recall elections. The "US election" is not a single event — it's a continuous, overlapping set of processes that never fully stops. Understanding any one race requires understanding the specific state system in which it takes place.

  • 48 What should you take away from all of this? +

    The US electoral system is not a coherent design — it's a series of historical compromises, accumulated amendments, and unintended consequences layered on top of an 18th-century framework built by men who did not anticipate political parties, mass democracy, or a continental nation of 330 million. It has been stretched, amended, and fought over for 235 years. Its contradictions — popular democracy and elite filtration, majority rule and minority protection, national unity and state sovereignty — have never been fully resolved. They are the ongoing argument of American politics. Final thought: Every element of this system — who can vote, how votes are counted, who draws the maps, who confirms the judges — was decided by people using political power. It can be changed by people using political power. The question is always: whose power, and to what end?
