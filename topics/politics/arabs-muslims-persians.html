Arabs, Muslims,
Persians & Islamists

These words are used as if they mean the same thing. They don't. Arab is an ethnicity. Muslim is a religion. Islamist is a political ideology. Persian is an ethnicity. Getting them right doesn't just improve your vocabulary — it changes how you read every conflict in the Middle East.

This is the complete guide. From Abraham to Hamas, from Sharia to the Saudi-Iran rivalry.

Before you start: Arab is an ethnicity. Muslim is a religion. Islamist is a political ideology. Persian is an ethnicity. Keep the categories separate and almost everything else becomes clearer.

  • Part 1

    Ethnicity: Arabs, Persians, and the others

  • 01Who are the Arabs?+

    Arabs are an ethnic group defined by language and culture, not religion. An Arab is someone for whom Arabic is the native language and who identifies with Arab culture. They are spread across 22 countries — from Morocco and Algeria in the west to Iraq and the Gulf states in the east. Arab identity was greatly unified by the 7th-century Islamic conquests, which spread Arabic as a language of religion and commerce. But not everyone conquered became Arab — the Persians are the clearest proof of that.Being Arab is about language and culture. It has nothing to do with religion. There are Muslim Arabs, Christian Arabs, and historically Jewish Arabs.

  • 02Who are the Persians — and why are Iranians not Arabs?+

    Persians are a completely different ethnic group with roots in ancient Iran. Their language, Farsi, belongs to the Indo-European family — entirely unrelated to Arabic. Iran was conquered by Arab armies in the 7th century and converted to Islam, but Iranians never became Arabs. They absorbed Islam, produced some of its greatest scholars and poets, and maintained a distinct civilizational identity stretching back 2,500 years.Calling an Iranian an Arab is not just inaccurate — it is genuinely offensive. Farsi and Arabic are as different as Greek and Hebrew. Both are in the region; they are completely unrelated.

  • 03What other ethnic groups live in the Middle East?+

    The region is far more diverse than it appears on a political map. The Kurds number roughly 30–40 million across Turkey, Iraq, Syria, and Iran — one of the world's largest ethnic groups without a state of their own. Turks dominate Turkey and Azerbaijan. Berbers (Amazigh) are the indigenous people of North Africa, distinct from the Arabs who arrived with the Islamic conquests. The region also contains Armenians, Assyrians, Yazidis, Druze, and many others — each with distinct languages, histories, and identities that predate both Islam and modern borders.Ask yourself: when a news anchor says "the Arab world reacted," which of these dozens of distinct ethnic groups is actually being described?

  • 04Why do modern borders not match the peoples living within them?+

    Because most of them were drawn by Britain and France after World War I, in the 1916 Sykes-Picot Agreement, with very little regard for who actually lived where. Iraq was created by combining three Ottoman provinces — Sunni Arabs, Shia Arabs, and Kurds — with no shared political tradition. Lebanon was built around a fragile sectarian balance that has never been fully stable. Syria, Jordan, and Kuwait all emerged from these arbitrary lines. The violence that has erupted repeatedly in these countries is, in part, the consequence of states built against the grain of their populations.Key insight: Many conflicts described as "ancient religious hatreds" are also — or primarily — the result of colonial borders imposed a century ago by outsiders who had little knowledge of the region.

  • Part 2

    Islam: what the religion actually is

  • 05What is Islam, and when did it begin?+

    Islam is a monotheistic religion founded in the 7th century CE in the Arabian Peninsula. The Prophet Muhammad, Muslims believe, received divine revelation — the Quran — from God (Allah) through the Angel Gabriel over approximately 23 years. "Islam" means submission to God; a Muslim is "one who submits." Islam spread with extraordinary speed: within a century of Muhammad's death in 632 CE, the Arab-Islamic empire stretched from Spain to Central Asia. Today Islam has approximately 1.9 billion followers — the world's second-largest religion.Key insight: Most Muslims are not Arab. Indonesia has more Muslims than any Arab country. Islam is a universal religion that transcended its Arab origins almost immediately after it began.

  • 06What are the Five Pillars of Islam?+

    The Five Pillars are the core religious duties of every Muslim: the Shahada (declaration of faith — "There is no god but God, and Muhammad is his messenger"), Salat (prayer five times a day, facing Mecca), Zakat (charitable giving, typically 2.5% of savings annually), Sawm (fasting during Ramadan), and Hajj (pilgrimage to Mecca at least once in a lifetime, for those able). These are shared by both Sunnis and Shia, despite their theological differences.The Five Pillars explain why Islam organizes daily life so completely. Prayer five times a day, fasting for a month, giving a percentage of wealth — these are not occasional observances. They structure time, body, and money year-round.

  • 07Are all Arabs Muslim?+

    No. The Arab world has a significant Christian minority. Lebanon has a large Christian population — Maronite Catholics, Greek Orthodox, and others — that was actually a majority as recently as the mid-20th century. Egypt has the Copts, one of the oldest Christian communities in the world, comprising around 10% of the population. Syria, Jordan, and Iraq also have sizable Christian Arab communities. Arab Christianity predates Islam by six centuries and traces directly to the earliest followers of Jesus.The conflation of "Arab" with "Muslim" erases millions of Arab Christians who have been in the region for two thousand years and whose presence predates Islam entirely.

  • 08What is the Quran, and how does it relate to the Bible?+

    The Quran is Islam's central text — Muslims believe it is the literal word of God as revealed to Muhammad. It contains many of the same figures as the Bible: Abraham, Moses, Jesus, and Mary all appear. Islam sees itself as the final revelation in a line that began with Abraham. In the Islamic view, earlier scriptures were genuine revelations from the same God but were corrupted or misinterpreted over time. Muhammad is the final prophet, the "seal of the prophets," sent to restore the original message.Islam, Christianity, and Judaism share enormous common ground. They worship the same God, revere many of the same prophets, and trace themselves to Abraham. The differences are about revelation and interpretation — not different gods.

  • 09Why are Mecca, Medina, and Jerusalem all holy in Islam?+

    Mecca is the holiest city — birthplace of Muhammad and site of the Kaaba, which Muslims face in prayer and circumambulate during Hajj. Muslims believe Abraham and Ishmael built the original Kaaba. Medina is where Muhammad fled in 622 CE (the Hijra — year one of the Islamic calendar) and where he built the first Muslim community. Jerusalem is Islam's third holiest city because of the Al-Aqsa Mosque and the Dome of the Rock, from which Muslims believe Muhammad ascended to heaven on the Night Journey.Ask yourself: when politicians debate "control of Jerusalem," they are debating control of a city considered sacred by three world religions simultaneously. That is not just a real estate dispute.

  • Part 3

    Islamism: when religion becomes political ideology

  • 10What is Islamism — and how is it different from Islam?+

    Islam is a religion. Islamism is a political ideology holding that Islamic principles should govern society, law, and the state. An Islamist is not simply a devout Muslim — millions of devout Muslims are explicitly opposed to Islamism. Islamism emerged in the 20th century, partly as a reaction to colonialism and Western cultural dominance. It exists on a wide spectrum — from democratic parties that participate in elections to revolutionary movements that reject all non-Islamic authority, to jihadist groups that embrace violence.Calling a Muslim an Islamist is like calling a Christian a theocrat. It may be true of some, but it describes a specific political position — not religious belief in general. Most Muslims worldwide do not want an Islamist state.

  • 11What is the Muslim Brotherhood?+

    The Muslim Brotherhood, founded in Egypt in 1928, was one of the first organized Islamist movements. Its core idea: Islam is not just a religion but a complete way of life that should govern politics and law. It spawned affiliated movements across the Arab world — including Hamas in Palestine. Egypt's government considers it a terrorist organization; other countries treat affiliated parties as legitimate political actors. Its intellectual legacy is enormous, shaping almost all modern Islamist thought.Key insight: The Muslim Brotherhood is not one coherent organization — it is a transnational network with wildly different national branches. The Jordanian Brotherhood participates in elections; Hamas fights a war. The common thread is ideology, not tactics.

  • 12What is Salafism — and what is Wahhabism?+

    Salafism is a conservative movement within Sunni Islam advocating a return to the practices of the first three generations of Muslims. Most Salafis are quietist — focused on personal piety, not politics. Wahhabism is a specific, state-sponsored variant of Salafism developed in 18th-century Arabia and adopted by the Saudi state. Saudi Arabia has spent hundreds of billions of dollars exporting Wahhabi thought globally through mosques, schools, and religious institutions — spreading one of the most conservative interpretations of Islam to communities worldwide.The Saudi export of Wahhabism is one of the most consequential and least discussed stories in modern geopolitics. Many of the conservative religious attitudes now prevalent in Muslim communities from Indonesia to Europe trace directly to Saudi-funded institutions.

  • 13What is Jihadism — and what does "jihad" actually mean?+

    The Arabic word "jihad" means "struggle" or "effort." In Islamic theology it has two primary meanings: the greater jihad — the internal spiritual struggle to live a good Muslim life — and the lesser jihad — armed struggle in defense of the Muslim community. Most Islamic scholars historically emphasized the spiritual dimension. Jihadism is a specific modern ideology that treats armed struggle as a religious obligation and uses it offensively. Al-Qaeda, ISIS, and similar groups are jihadist — a tiny minority explicitly condemned by the vast majority of Muslim scholars and governments.The hierarchy runs: Islam (1.9 billion) → Islamism → Salafism → Jihadism. Conflating any level with the one above it is the single most common analytical error in Western coverage of the Middle East.

  • Part 4

    Sunni and Shia: a 1,400-year fracture

  • 14How did the Sunni-Shia split begin?+

    When Muhammad died in 632 CE, he left no clear instructions about who should lead the Muslim community. One faction chose Abu Bakr, his close companion — they became the Sunnis. Another believed leadership rightfully belonged to Ali ibn Abi Talib, Muhammad's cousin and son-in-law — they became the Shia (from "Shi'at Ali" — the party of Ali). The split became permanent at the Battle of Karbala in 680 CE, when Ali's son Hussein was killed by the forces of the Umayyad caliph.The split began as a political dispute about succession. Over 1,400 years it developed into distinct theological traditions, legal schools, and geopolitical blocs. Today about 85–90% of Muslims are Sunni; 10–15% are Shia.

  • 15What is Karbala, and why does it still matter?+

    Karbala is a city in modern Iraq where Hussein ibn Ali, grandson of Muhammad, was killed in 680 CE by the vastly larger army of the Umayyad caliph. For Shia Muslims, Karbala is a foundational tragedy — an act of injustice and martyrdom that defines Shia identity. It is commemorated annually as Ashura, with mourning processions and passion plays. The themes of Karbala — the righteous few standing against an unjust power, the willingness to die for principle — have given Shia political movements including Hezbollah and the Iranian Revolution their emotional and theological vocabulary.Karbala is not ancient history for Shia Muslims — it is a living wound. Understanding this explains why Shia political movements have such intense emotional cohesion, and why the language of martyrdom resonates so powerfully.

  • 16Where are Sunnis and Shia today — and why does it matter geopolitically?+

    Sunnis dominate most of the Muslim world: Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Turkey, Pakistan, Indonesia. Shia Islam is the majority in Iran, Iraq, and Bahrain, and a significant minority in Lebanon, Yemen, Kuwait, and eastern Saudi Arabia. Iran is the main Shia state power; Saudi Arabia is the main Sunni state power. Their rivalry — simultaneously theological, ethnic (Arab vs Persian), and geopolitical — has shaped proxy wars for decades. Yemen, Lebanon, Iraq's fragmentation, and the Syrian war all carry a Sunni-Shia dimension alongside the many other factors driving them.Ask yourself: the Iran-Saudi rivalry is simultaneously a theological dispute, an ethnic competition between Arabs and Persians, and a raw power struggle for regional dominance. Remove any one dimension and the conflict still exists. That is what makes it so durable.

  • Part 5

    Sharia: what the word actually means

  • 17What is Sharia?+

    Sharia (literally "the path to water") is the Islamic moral and legal code derived from the Quran and the Hadith — the recorded sayings and actions of Muhammad. It covers religious obligations, family law, commercial law, criminal law, and personal ethics. In Western media it usually appears in the context of its criminal provisions — including corporal punishment for certain offenses. These provisions exist in the texts, but their application varies enormously across Muslim countries and communities.Key insight: Sharia is not a single fixed legal code you switch on or off. It is a vast tradition of jurisprudence with four major Sunni legal schools and distinct Shia traditions. "Sharia" means something different in Saudi Arabia, Egypt, Indonesia, and a Muslim family in France.

  • 18Where is Sharia applied today — and how differently?+

    The range is enormous. Saudi Arabia applies a comprehensive version in both criminal and family law. Iran applies Sharia through a unique system blending Islamic jurisprudence with clerical political structure. Egypt applies it in personal status law but uses a civil code for criminal law. Indonesia — the world's largest Muslim country — has a largely secular legal system with Sharia applicable only in the special province of Aceh. Turkey has a fully secular legal system with no Sharia in state courts at all.Ask yourself: when a politician warns about "Sharia law," which version are they describing? Saudi Arabia's, Indonesia's, or the personal prayer schedule of a Muslim in Manchester?

  • 19Do all Muslims want Sharia as state law?+

    No — and the variation is far wider than Western coverage suggests. In Afghanistan and Pakistan, large majorities favor Sharia as official state law; in Turkey, Kazakhstan, and Albania, strong majorities oppose it. The desire to live personally by Islamic moral principles — praying, fasting, eating halal — is near-universal among observant Muslims and has nothing to do with wanting a theocratic state. These are different questions entirely.Personal religious observance and political Islamism are completely separate dimensions. A Muslim who prays five times a day and opposes an Islamist government is not a contradiction — that describes the majority of Muslims worldwide.

  • Part 6

    Abraham: the shared ancestor

  • 20How are Islam, Christianity, and Judaism related through Abraham?+

    All three are "Abrahamic religions" — they trace their spiritual lineage to Abraham (Ibrahim in Arabic), the patriarch who entered into a covenant with God. Judaism descends through Abraham's son Isaac. Christianity inherits this tradition and adds that Jesus fulfills God's promises to Abraham. Islam regards Muhammad as the final prophet in a line beginning with Abraham — and Muslims believe Abraham and his son Ishmael built the Kaaba in Mecca, Islam's holiest site. They worship the same God.Key insight: The conflicts between Israel and the Arab world, or between the West and the Islamic world, are not clashes between people who worship different gods. The conflicts are about land, politics, history, and power — dressed in theological language.

  • 21What is the significance of Ishmael versus Isaac?+

    In the Hebrew Bible, God's covenant passes through Isaac, Abraham's son with his wife Sarah. Ishmael, born of Hagar, is sent away but blessed by God and traditionally associated with the Arab peoples. In Islam, Ishmael is a full prophet — and the one whom Abraham nearly sacrificed. Muslims believe this was Ishmael, not Isaac. The Eid al-Adha holiday commemorates this event. This single divergence — which son carries the primary covenant — maps onto territorial and political claims about the Holy Land that are still contested today.Two religions, one patriarch, two different sons. The question of which line carries God's primary blessing has underwritten centuries of competing claims over the same strip of land.

  • 22Why is Jerusalem holy to all three religions?+

    For Jews, Jerusalem is the site of the First and Second Temples — the Western Wall is the holiest place where Jews can pray today. For Christians, Jerusalem is where Jesus was crucified, buried, and according to Christian belief, resurrected — the Church of the Holy Sepulchre marks these sites. For Muslims, Jerusalem contains the Al-Aqsa Mosque (Islam's third holiest site) and the Dome of the Rock, from which Muhammad ascended to heaven. All of these claims concentrate on the same small hilltop — the Temple Mount to Jews, the Haram al-Sharif to Muslims.No other city on earth is simultaneously the most contested ground in three of the world's largest religions. This is not an accident of geography — it is the accumulated weight of 3,000 years of sacred history converging on one place.

Geographic overview — Arab world vs Muslim world

  • Part 7

    Israel and Palestine

  • 23Why does Israel exist where it does?+

    The modern state of Israel was established in 1948, but the Jewish connection to the land is several thousand years old. Jewish kingdoms existed there roughly 1000–600 BCE. The Romans destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE and dispersed much of the Jewish population. For 2,000 years, Jewish communities worldwide maintained an orientation toward Jerusalem in prayer and preserved the aspiration to return. Modern Zionism, a secular nationalist movement that emerged in the late 19th century in response to European antisemitism, gave this longing political form. The Holocaust gave it urgency. The UN partition plan of 1947 divided British Mandatory Palestine between a Jewish and an Arab state; Israel declared independence in 1948; the Arab states rejected the partition and attacked.Israel's location is not arbitrary. It reflects the oldest continuously documented connection between a people and a place in recorded history. The tragedy is that the Palestinians also had deep roots in the same land — and both claims are historically real.

  • 24Who are the Palestinians?+

    Palestinians are the Arab people — predominantly Muslim, with a significant Christian minority — whose families lived in the territory of Mandatory Palestine before the establishment of Israel. In 1948, the war that Israelis call the War of Independence and Palestinians call the Nakba ("catastrophe") resulted in the displacement of approximately 700,000 Palestinians. Their descendants — now numbering in the millions — live in Gaza, the West Bank, Jordan, Lebanon, Syria, and in diaspora communities worldwide. Palestinians are divided politically between the Palestinian Authority (which governs parts of the West Bank) and Hamas (which governs Gaza).The Palestinian refugee question is one of the oldest unresolved displacement crises in the world. No solution exists that does not require one side to give up something it considers existential.

  • 25Why do Arab states and Iran oppose Israel — and are their reasons the same?+

    Arab opposition began as a nationalist and territorial conflict over Palestinian displacement. Over time many Arab states pragmatically accepted Israel's existence: Egypt and Jordan signed peace treaties, and the 2020 Abraham Accords normalized relations with the UAE, Bahrain, Sudan, and Morocco. Iran's opposition is structurally different: the Islamic Republic, founded in 1979, made anti-Zionism a cornerstone of its founding ideology — simultaneously a religious statement, a tool for projecting regional influence through proxies like Hezbollah and Hamas, and a way of claiming moral leadership in the Muslim world.Arab opposition to Israel has fragmented and, in several cases, reversed. Iranian opposition is ideologically structural — built into the founding identity of the state. They are not the same thing.

  • 26What is the two-state solution — and why hasn't it happened?+

    The two-state solution proposes an independent Palestinian state in the West Bank and Gaza alongside Israel. It has been the official position of most international actors for decades. It has not happened because of compounding obstacles: Israeli settlement expansion in the West Bank (now home to over 700,000 settlers) has made territorial contiguity for a Palestinian state increasingly difficult; Palestinian political division between the PA and Hamas means there is no unified negotiating partner; domestic politics on both sides have been captured by factions that oppose compromise; and repeated negotiations — in 2000 and 2008 — collapsed on Jerusalem, refugees, and borders.The two-state solution is simultaneously the only widely accepted peace framework and the option that powerful actors on both sides are systematically making less possible. This is not a paradox — it is a political choice.

  • Part 8

    Modern armed groups

  • 27What is Hamas?+

    Hamas (an acronym for "Islamic Resistance Movement") is a Palestinian Islamist organization that emerged in 1987 as an offshoot of the Muslim Brotherhood. It won Palestinian legislative elections in 2006 and took full control of Gaza in 2007. Hamas simultaneously operates as a government (providing schools, hospitals, and services), a political movement, and a military organization that has carried out attacks against Israel. It is designated a terrorist organization by the United States, the European Union, and others. It receives significant financial and military support from Iran.Hamas is impossible to understand through a single lens. It is at once a resistance movement, a governing authority, a terrorist organization, and an Iranian proxy — all simultaneously. Reducing it to any one of these is politically motivated, not analytically accurate.

  • 28What is Hezbollah?+

    Hezbollah ("Party of God") is a Lebanese Shia organization founded in 1982 with direct Iranian support following Israel's invasion of Lebanon. It is simultaneously a political party (with seats in the Lebanese parliament), a social welfare network (running hospitals, schools, and services in Shia communities), and one of the most heavily armed non-state militaries in the world — possessing more missiles than most national armies. It is closely integrated into Iran's regional proxy network and has deployed forces to support Assad in Syria, Houthi rebels in Yemen, and other Iran-aligned groups across the region.Hezbollah's power comes precisely from the fact that it cannot be separated from Lebanon itself. This is by design — it is Iran's model of embedding proxy forces so deeply into host states that they become untouchable.

  • 29What is ISIS / Daesh?+

    ISIS (Islamic State of Iraq and Syria, also known as Daesh — its Arabic acronym) is a jihadist group that emerged from post-invasion Iraq, drawing on al-Qaeda's network and the grievances of Sunni Arabs excluded by Iraq's Shia-dominated post-Saddam government. At its peak in 2014, ISIS controlled territory roughly the size of the United Kingdom across Iraq and Syria, declared a "caliphate," and enforced a brutal version of Sharia through mass executions, slavery, and destruction of historical sites. It was militarily defeated by 2019 through a coalition that included the US, Kurdish forces, Iran, and Russia — a rare alignment of otherwise hostile parties.ISIS was explicitly condemned as un-Islamic by the overwhelming majority of Muslim scholars worldwide. Its caliphate attracted perhaps 40,000 foreign fighters from 100 countries — a tiny fraction of 1% of global Muslims. The enemy of ISIS and the enemy of the West are not the same thing.

  • 30What is the Taliban?+

    The Taliban ("students" in Pashto) is an Islamist movement that emerged in Afghanistan in the 1990s from Pashtun-dominated mujahideen networks that had fought the Soviet occupation. They governed Afghanistan from 1996 to 2001, enforcing an extreme interpretation of Sharia — banning girls' education, music, and television. After being ousted by the US-led invasion following September 11, they waged a 20-year insurgency and returned to power in 2021 when the US withdrew. The Taliban are ethnically Pashtun, nationally Afghan, and ideologically Deobandi Salafist — distinct from, and sometimes in conflict with, ISIS and Al-Qaeda, despite surface ideological similarities.The Taliban, ISIS, Al-Qaeda, and Hamas are all broadly Islamist — but they differ in ethnicity, ideology, strategy, and geopolitical alignment in ways that are critical for understanding each one. Conflating them misses everything important.

  • Part 9

    Key states and powers

  • 31How does Iran actually work — who is really in charge?+

    Iran is an Islamic Republic with a unique dual power structure. There is an elected President and parliament — but above them stands the Supreme Leader (currently Ali Khamenei), an unelected religious authority with final say over all major decisions including foreign policy, the military, and the nuclear program. The Revolutionary Guards (IRGC) are a parallel military structure that controls vast economic interests and runs the regional proxy network. Elected officials have real power in domestic matters but operate within hard limits set by the clerical establishment.When you hear about negotiations with Iran, it is worth asking: which Iran? The elected president's Iran, which wants trade and recognition? Or the Supreme Leader's Iran, which defines itself through opposition to the US and Israel? These are not the same government.

  • 32What is Saudi Arabia's role — and why does the West support it?+

    Saudi Arabia holds about 17% of the world's proven oil reserves, controls the swing production capacity of OPEC, and hosts two of Islam's three holiest cities (Mecca and Medina). The Saudi state was built on a foundational pact: political power for the Al Saud royal family, religious influence for the Wahhabi clerical establishment. The West supports Saudi Arabia primarily for energy security, weapons sales, and regional stability against Iranian influence — despite Saudi Arabia's authoritarian governance, human rights record, and the documented global export of conservative Islamist ideology.The relationship between Western democracies and Saudi Arabia is a masterclass in the subordination of values to interests. Every Western government knows what the Saudi state does. They have decided the oil and the strategic position are worth more than the principles.

  • 33Is Turkey secular or Islamist?+

    Both — and the tension between the two is one of the defining stories of modern Turkish politics. Atatürk founded the modern Republic of Turkey in 1923 on explicitly secular foundations, abolishing the caliphate and replacing Islamic law with European legal codes. For decades, the military acted as a guardian of this secularism. Since 2002, President Erdoğan and his AKP party have governed Turkey, progressively rolling back secularist institutions, reintroducing Islamic references into public life, and converting the Hagia Sophia from a museum back to a mosque. Turkey today is neither strictly secular nor Islamist — it is somewhere in between, and shifting.Turkey is the most important test case for whether a Muslim-majority democracy can sustain secular governance over the long term. The answer, so far, is: with great difficulty.

  • 34What is the "Axis of Resistance"?+

    The "Axis of Resistance" is the name used by Iran and its allies for the network of state and non-state actors aligned with Iran against Israel and US influence in the region. It includes Iran, the Syrian government of Assad (Iran's only Arab state ally), Hezbollah in Lebanon, Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad in Gaza, the Houthi movement in Yemen, and various Shia militias in Iraq. Not all members are Shia — Hamas is Sunni — but all are united by opposition to Israel and the US-aligned regional order. The axis is held together by Iranian money, weapons, training, and ideology.The Axis of Resistance is Iran's answer to being surrounded by hostile or US-aligned states. By embedding allied forces in Lebanon, Gaza, Yemen, and Iraq, Iran ensures any attack on Iran would trigger responses from multiple fronts simultaneously. It is a deterrence strategy dressed in ideological language.

  • Part 10

    Language: words you hear and what they mean

  • 35What is the difference between an Imam, an Ayatollah, a Caliph, and a Mufti?+

    An Imam (in Sunni usage) is simply a prayer leader — anyone who leads communal prayer in a mosque. In Shia Islam, "Imam" has far greater theological weight: the twelve Imams are the rightful successors of Muhammad, the last of whom is believed to be in occultation and will return. An Ayatollah is a senior Shia cleric — literally "sign of God" — who has achieved the highest level of Islamic legal scholarship. A Caliph is the political and religious leader of the Muslim community — a title that died with the Ottoman caliphate in 1924 and was briefly and controversially claimed by ISIS. A Mufti is a religious scholar who issues fatwas — legal opinions on Islamic questions.These titles carry enormous authority in their respective communities and zero authority outside them. Using them interchangeably or incorrectly signals to every informed Muslim that the speaker does not understand what they are talking about.

  • 36What is a mosque, a madrasa, and a fatwa?+

    A mosque (masjid) is a place of Islamic worship — the equivalent of a church or synagogue. A madrasa is an Islamic school, traditionally focused on Quranic memorization and Islamic jurisprudence. In Pakistan and Afghanistan some madrasas became recruiting grounds for jihadist movements; in most of the Muslim world they are simply religious schools. A fatwa is a formal legal opinion issued by a qualified scholar on a question of Islamic law. Fatwas are not binding on all Muslims — they are opinions from a scholar within a specific legal tradition. The most famous fatwa in recent Western awareness is Khomeini's 1989 call for the killing of Salman Rushdie — an exceptional act, not representative of how fatwas normally function.Most fatwas are mundane legal opinions about diet, finance, or family law. The image of the fatwa as a death sentence is a product of one notorious exception. Using that exception to define the institution is like defining all court orders as death warrants.

  • 37What is halal, haram, hijab, niqab, and burqa?+

    Halal means "permitted" in Islamic law — most commonly applied to food (meat slaughtered according to Islamic requirements, no pork or alcohol). Haram means "forbidden." Hijab refers to the principle of modesty in Islamic dress and, specifically, the headscarf worn by many Muslim women — ranging from voluntary expression of faith to state-mandated requirement (Iran) to state-banned garment (France in public institutions). A niqab covers the face but leaves the eyes uncovered. A burqa is a full-body covering with a mesh screen over the face — associated primarily with Afghan Pashtun culture and Taliban enforcement; it is not widely worn in most Muslim communities.Ask yourself: when a debate erupts about Muslim women's dress, who is speaking for those women? The debate is almost always conducted by outsiders — governments, journalists, politicians — about women whose own range of views on the subject is rarely centered in the conversation.

  • 38What is Ramadan, and what is Eid?+

    Ramadan is the ninth month of the Islamic lunar calendar, during which Muslims fast from dawn to sunset — abstaining from food, drink, and smoking. It commemorates the month in which the Quran was first revealed to Muhammad and is one of the Five Pillars. Eid al-Fitr ("Festival of Breaking the Fast") marks the end of Ramadan — three days of prayer, feasting, family visits, and giving to charity. Eid al-Adha ("Festival of the Sacrifice") comes two months later and commemorates Abraham's willingness to sacrifice his son — families who can afford to sacrifice an animal and distribute the meat to the poor.Ramadan and Eid are the most widely observed Islamic practices worldwide, cutting across all sectarian, ethnic, and political divides within Islam. Understanding them is basic literacy about how 1.9 billion people organize a significant part of their year.

  • Part 11

    Why they fight each other

  • 39Why do Muslim-majority countries fight each other if they share the same religion?+

    Because religion is rarely the only — or even the primary — driver of conflict. The Iran-Iraq War (1980–1988) was fought between two Muslim-majority states over border disputes, ethnic rivalry between Arabs and Persians, oil, and political fear. Saudi Arabia and Qatar have been in prolonged diplomatic conflict over foreign policy and media influence — both Sunni Arab states. Morocco and Algeria have a long-running dispute over the Western Sahara. These are territorial, ethnic, economic, and political conflicts that happen to involve Muslim actors.Shared religion does not prevent war, just as shared Christianity did not prevent centuries of European wars. The Thirty Years' War, the French Wars of Religion, the English Civil War — all fought between Christians. Geopolitics runs on interests. Religion provides the vocabulary, but rarely the original motivation.

  • 40What is the root of the Arab-Persian rivalry?+

    One of history's oldest cultural fault lines. The Arabs conquered the Persian Sassanid Empire in the 7th century — a conquest Iranians remember as a political trauma even though they adopted Islam. Iranians preserved their language, their calendar, their poetry (Hafez, Rumi, Ferdowsi), and a civilizational identity predating Islam by a millennium. The sense that Persians gave the Arabs culture while the Arabs gave them religion has never entirely faded. In modern geopolitics this rivalry expresses itself through the Saudi-Iran competition — ethnic, sectarian, and strategic all at once.The Arab-Persian rivalry predates Islam by centuries and has outlasted every attempt at pan-Islamic unity. It runs on ethnic, civilizational, sectarian, and geopolitical tracks simultaneously — which is why it is so durable and so resistant to resolution.

  • 41What role did colonialism play in creating today's conflicts?+

    An enormous one. The Sykes-Picot Agreement of 1916 between Britain and France divided the Arab Middle East into spheres of influence that became the borders of modern states — without meaningful consultation with the populations involved. Britain simultaneously promised the same land to multiple parties: the Balfour Declaration of 1917 promised a Jewish homeland in Palestine; the Hussein-McMahon correspondence had suggested Arab independence in the same territory. The betrayal of promises made to both Arabs and Jews during World War I left a legacy of grievance that shapes the conflict to this day.The instinct to say "these conflicts go back thousands of years" is both true and misleading. Many of the specific forms in which they are fought today — the borders, the states, the armed factions — were shaped or created by decisions made in London and Paris between 1916 and 1948.

  • 42Oil, power, religion: which actually drives the conflicts?+

    All three — and they are not separable. Oil gives states the financial power to fund militaries, buy foreign allies, and export ideology. Religion provides the vocabulary for mobilizing populations and legitimizing authority. Power — the raw competition for regional dominance — shapes which alliances form and which conflicts erupt. Saudi Arabia's export of Wahhabism would not have been possible without oil revenues. Iran's nuclear program is simultaneously about regime survival, regional deterrence, and national pride. Hamas's military capacity depends on Iranian money. Asking which factor is "really" driving any specific conflict is like asking which ingredient is "really" making the food taste bad when everything in the pot is off.Anyone who tells you these conflicts are "really just about religion" or "really just about oil" is selling you a simplified story. The whole point of these conflicts is that they operate on multiple dimensions at once. That is what makes them so difficult to resolve.

  • Part 12

    The big picture

  • 43Is peace in the Middle East possible?+

    Possible, yes. Imminent, no. The Abraham Accords showed that Arab states can normalize with Israel when their strategic interests align — and further normalization, potentially including Saudi Arabia, remains a live possibility. The Palestinian question remains the deepest structural obstacle. No settlement that denies Palestinian political aspirations has ever held; no Arab or Muslim government can fully normalize with Israel in the face of continued occupation without severe domestic consequences. What the region lacks is not a peace formula — many of those exist on paper — but the political will to bear the domestic cost of implementing one.The Middle East is not condemned to perpetual conflict. The economic incentives for stability are enormous; the human cost of instability is incalculable. Egypt and Israel at peace since 1979 seemed impossible in 1973. Cynicism is earned. It is also not the whole picture.

  • 44What does the West consistently get wrong about this region?+

    Several things, consistently. First, conflating the categories in this article — treating Arab, Muslim, and Islamist as synonymous. Second, reading the region through a binary lens (pro-West vs anti-West, secular vs religious) that misses the complex local dynamics driving most conflicts. Third, assuming Western liberal democracy is the natural endpoint of all political development and that its absence reflects a cultural failure rather than a history of external interference. Fourth, overestimating the role of ideology and underestimating the role of political economy — poverty, corruption, and authoritarian governance drive radicalization more reliably than theology. Fifth, focusing on the symptoms (terrorism, refugee flows) rather than the structural causes (occupation, proxy wars, failing states).The West has intervened in the Middle East repeatedly over the last century — drawing borders, installing governments, fighting wars, providing weapons — and has rarely acknowledged the consequences of those interventions in its analysis of what went wrong. This is not accident; it is a form of institutional forgetting.

  • 45What is the single most important thing to understand about all of this?+

    That the categories matter, and that complexity is not a sign that something is unknowable — it is an invitation to look more carefully. Arab is an ethnicity. Muslim is a religion. Islamist is a political ideology. Persian is an ethnicity. Sunni and Shia are religious sects. Hamas is a Palestinian organization. Hezbollah is Lebanese. ISIS was a jihadist state project. The Taliban is Afghan. Iran is a Shia Persian theocracy. Saudi Arabia is a Sunni Arab monarchy. These are different things. Understanding the difference doesn't require specialist knowledge — it requires the willingness to hold more than two categories in your head at once. Key insight: The question to ask about any conflict in the Middle East is never "is this religious or political?" It is almost always both — layered, historical, economic, and personal at the same time. Anyone telling you it is simple is selling something. Final thought: The people most harmed by these misunderstandings are usually the ones being misunderstood.
