Part 1

What Are They?

  • 01 What are rare earth minerals? ↓

    A group of 17 metallic elements with extraordinary magnetic, luminescent, and electrochemical properties. They don't look like much — most are dull grey metals. But their properties make them irreplaceable in modern technology. The name is misleading. "Rare earth" is a historical accident — early chemists found them hard to isolate, not because they were scarce, but because they cluster together and resist separation.

  • 02 Are they actually rare? +

    Not exactly. Most rare earth elements are relatively abundant in the Earth's crust — cerium is more common than copper. The problem is concentration: they are almost never found in deposits rich enough to mine economically. And processing them is enormously complex. Ask yourself: if they're not rare, why does one country control 85% of the supply? The answer is not geology — it's decades of strategic investment.

  • 03 How many rare earth elements exist? +

    17 — including elements like neodymium, dysprosium, lanthanum, cerium, and praseodymium. Each has different properties and different uses. Neodymium makes the strongest permanent magnets known. Europium makes screens glow red. Yttrium makes them glow green.

  • 04 Where are they found? +

    They exist on every continent. China has the largest known reserves, but significant deposits exist in Australia, Russia, India, Brazil, and across Africa. The US has large reserves too — particularly in California and Wyoming. Having reserves and being able to mine and process them are very different things.

  • 05 Why do they have such special properties? +

    Their electron configuration — the way electrons are arranged around the nucleus — gives them unusual magnetic and optical behaviors. Neodymium magnets, for example, are 10 times stronger than regular iron magnets of the same size. This can't be replicated with other materials. Physics, not politics, is why rare earths are irreplaceable. You can't negotiate with the periodic table.

  • 06 From when have we known about them? +

    The first rare earth element was isolated in 1787. But they remained laboratory curiosities for nearly two centuries. It wasn't until the 1980s and 1990s — with the rise of consumer electronics — that demand exploded and they became strategically critical.

  • 07 What everyday products use rare earth minerals? +

    Your smartphone contains up to 16 rare earth elements. Your laptop, headphones, electric car motor, wind turbine, MRI machine, LED lights, camera lens, and hard drive all depend on them. The F-35 fighter jet requires nearly 920 pounds of rare earth materials. You are not reading about something abstract. You are holding rare earth minerals right now.

  • 08 Why can't we just use something else? +

    Because nothing else works as well. Researchers have tried for decades to find substitutes for rare earth magnets in electric motors and wind turbines. The alternatives are heavier, weaker, or far more expensive. For many applications, rare earths are simply irreplaceable with current technology.

  • Part 2

    Why Is Everyone Talking About Them Now?

  • 09 Why are rare earths suddenly everywhere in the news? +

    Three things converged: the green energy transition (which requires massive amounts of rare earths), the AI revolution (which requires rare earth-dependent chips and hardware), and the US-China trade war (which made China's dominance a national security issue rather than just an economic one). Rare earths were always strategically important. What changed is that the West finally started paying attention.

  • 10 What is the connection to electric vehicles? +

    Every electric vehicle motor uses neodymium-iron-boron magnets — the most powerful permanent magnets in existence. A single EV requires several kilograms of rare earth elements. The global push for EVs has made demand for rare earths skyrocket. Ask yourself: we are transitioning away from oil dependency — and directly into rare earth dependency. Have we solved the problem, or just changed who controls the resource?

  • 11 What is the connection to AI and semiconductors? +

    AI requires enormous computing power — which requires data centers — which require cooling systems, hard drives, and specialized chips. All of these depend on rare earth elements. The rare earth supply chain runs directly underneath the AI revolution.

  • 12 What is the connection to military technology? +

    Guided missiles, radar systems, night vision equipment, jet engines, submarines, satellite communications — all require rare earth elements. The F-35 fighter needs 920 pounds of them. A Virginia-class submarine needs 9,200 pounds. Modern warfare runs on rare earths. A country that controls rare earths controls the inputs for its adversary's weapons. That is not a trade issue. That is a security issue.

  • 13 How dependent is the modern economy on rare earths? +

    Completely. Remove rare earths from the supply chain and you lose: electric vehicles, wind turbines, most consumer electronics, medical imaging equipment, defense systems, and the hardware running the AI industry. The modern economy does not function without them.

  • 14 Which industries would collapse without them? +

    In the short term: defense, consumer electronics, and medical technology. In the medium term: the entire green energy transition — wind power and electric vehicles would grind to a halt. In the long term: the AI industry and any technology depending on miniaturized high-performance components. Ask yourself: we talk about energy security and food security. Why don't we talk about rare earth security?

  • Part 3

    Who Controls the World?

  • 15 Who controls global rare earth production? +

    China, overwhelmingly. It produces roughly 85% of the world's rare earth minerals and controls an even higher percentage of processing capacity — the ability to refine raw ore into usable materials. Mining is only half the battle; processing is where China's dominance is almost total.

  • 16 How dominant is China exactly? +

    Even countries that mine their own rare earths often send the ore to China for processing — because China has built the refining infrastructure that no one else has. Australia mines rare earths and ships them to China. The US has a mine in California that sends its ore to China. The West didn't just let China mine more. It let China build the entire processing industry. That is the dependency that is almost impossible to reverse quickly.

  • 17 How did China get this advantage? +

    Deliberate, patient, decades-long strategy. In the 1980s, Deng Xiaoping said: "The Middle East has oil. China has rare earths." Beijing subsidized production, accepted environmental damage, and undercut Western competitors on price until they shut down. It wasn't an accident. It was a plan. China played a 40-year game. The West played a quarterly earnings game. The results speak for themselves.

  • 18 What is the "rare earth weapon"? +

    China's ability to restrict or cut off rare earth exports as geopolitical leverage — essentially holding modern industry hostage. Because processing is so centralized, even countries with their own deposits would struggle to function without Chinese refineries. The weapon is not the minerals. It is the chokepoint.

  • 19 Has China ever used this weapon? +

    Yes — in 2010, during a territorial dispute with Japan over the Senkaku/Diaoyu Islands. China halted rare earth exports to Japan for weeks. Japanese manufacturers that depended on rare earths were immediately affected. China denied it was political. Everyone knew it was. China has used the weapon once — and only once. The threat that it might use it again is powerful enough that it rarely needs to.

  • 20 Why did the West let this happen? +

    Short-termism. Chinese rare earths were cheap. Western mining and processing was expensive and environmentally damaging. Companies chose cheap. Governments let them. No one wanted to pay the cost of strategic independence when the market offered a cheaper option. Ask yourself: how many strategic vulnerabilities has the West created by always choosing the cheapest option in the short term?

  • 21 What happened with Japan in 2010 — and what did Japan do about it? +

    After China's export restrictions, Japan responded with the most serious rare earth diversification effort in history. It invested in mines in Australia and Canada, funded recycling technology, and worked to reduce rare earth content in its products. A decade later, Japan is significantly less dependent on China than it was. Japan showed it's possible to reduce dependence — but it took a crisis, a decade, and billions of dollars to do it.

  • 22 What does this mean for the US and Europe? +

    Strategic vulnerability. The US depends on China for the inputs to build its own weapons systems. Europe depends on China for the materials needed for its green transition. Both are racing to reduce this dependency — but building new supply chains takes a decade, and China has a 40-year head start.

  • Part 4

    The Geopolitical War

  • 23 Why are rare earths called "the new oil"? +

    Because the 20th century ran on oil — and wars were fought over it, alliances built around it, and economies shaped by who controlled it. The 21st century runs on technology — and rare earths are to technology what oil was to industry. The geopolitics are strikingly similar. The difference is that oil was distributed across many countries. Rare earth processing is concentrated in one. That makes it more dangerous, not less.

  • 24 Can the US become self-sufficient in rare earths? +

    Possibly — but not quickly. The US has significant deposits, and the Mountain Pass mine in California is producing again. But the US has almost no domestic processing capacity. Building a rare earth refinery takes 10-15 years and billions of dollars. Self-sufficiency is a decade away at minimum.

  • 25 Why is rare earth mining so difficult? +

    Three reasons: the ore is low-grade (a lot of rock for a little metal), the separation process is chemically complex and produces toxic waste, and the environmental regulations in most Western countries make it extremely expensive. China accepted the environmental cost. The West largely hasn't. Ask yourself: do we want clean supply chains — or do we want to simply move the environmental damage to countries with weaker regulations?

  • 26 Which countries are trying to challenge China? +

    Australia is the most advanced alternative producer. Canada and Brazil have significant deposits being developed. The US is investing heavily. The EU has designated rare earths as critical raw materials and is funding domestic projects. But none are close to matching China's processing capacity.

  • 27 What is Africa's role? +

    Africa has enormous rare earth deposits — particularly in Tanzania, South Africa, Madagascar, and the DRC. China has been investing heavily in African mining for decades, often in exchange for infrastructure. The West is now competing for the same deposits, but China has a significant head start. The rare earth competition is also a competition for Africa. What looks like an infrastructure deal is often a strategic resource grab.

  • 28 What changed with the US-China trade war? +

    China explicitly threatened to cut off rare earth exports to the US in 2019 and again in 2025 as retaliation for tariffs and technology restrictions. Each time, markets reacted. The US government accelerated domestic production subsidies. The rare earth supply chain went from a background issue to a front-page crisis.

  • 29 How close are we to a technology or energy crisis over rare earths? +

    Closer than most people realize. Demand is growing faster than supply diversification. If China imposed even a partial export restriction today, the effects would be felt within months in defense, clean energy, and consumer electronics. There is almost no strategic stockpile in the West. The world has oil reserves measured in months or years. For rare earths, most Western countries have almost nothing in reserve.

  • Part 5

    The Future — and the Shock

  • 30 Can rare earths be recycled? +

    Yes — but currently less than 1% are. The technology exists but is expensive and not yet scaled. Japan leads in rare earth recycling research. A future where old smartphones and wind turbines become the "mines" of the 21st century is possible — but we are not there yet.

  • 31 Are there any substitutes? +

    For some applications, yes — but with significant performance penalties. Ferrite magnets can replace neodymium magnets in some motors, but they are much heavier and weaker. Researchers are working on rare earth-free motors, but they remain less efficient. For now, there is no substitute that matches rare earth performance. Science is working on alternatives. But technology transitions take decades — and the demand crisis is now.

  • 32 How long will supplies last? +

    Known reserves are estimated to last hundreds of years at current consumption rates. But consumption is not staying current — it is accelerating dramatically with EVs, wind turbines, and AI infrastructure. The question is not whether reserves exist. It is whether we can mine and process them fast enough.

  • 33 What would happen if China cut off the West completely? +

    In the short term: defense production would slow, EV manufacturing would be disrupted, and consumer electronics prices would spike. In the medium term: a scramble to open new mines and build processing capacity at enormous cost. In the long term: the West would adapt — but the transition would be painful and expensive. Ask yourself: China almost certainly knows this. So why hasn't it used the weapon fully? Because the disruption would also hurt China's export economy. Mutually assured economic destruction is a real deterrent.

  • 34 Could this trigger a war? +

    Not directly over rare earths — but they are a significant factor in the broader US-China competition that could lead to conflict. The rare earth dependency means the US military's ability to fight is partially dependent on Chinese supply chains. That is a strategic vulnerability that military planners take very seriously.

  • 35 What is the West doing to reduce dependence? +

    Multiple approaches simultaneously: subsidizing domestic mining, investing in processing capacity, funding recycling research, securing supply deals with friendly countries (Australia, Canada), and funding research into substitutes. The US Inflation Reduction Act included significant rare earth provisions. The EU's Critical Raw Materials Act does the same. All of this is happening 20 years too late. The question is whether it's happening fast enough.

  • 36 Will rare earths reshape global power? +

    They already are. The country that controls rare earth processing controls the inputs for the technologies that will define the next century — AI, clean energy, advanced weapons. If China maintains its dominance, it holds leverage over every major economy indefinitely. If the West diversifies successfully, the leverage diminishes. This is the defining industrial competition of the 21st century.

  • 37 How many rare earth elements are in your phone right now? +

    Up to 16 out of 17 rare earth elements can be found in a modern smartphone. Neodymium in the speakers and vibration motor. Europium and terbium in the screen. Lanthanum in the camera lens. Dysprosium in the battery. Your phone is a rare earth museum. The geopolitical crisis you just read about fits in your pocket.

  • 38 What would happen if rare earths disappeared from the market tomorrow? +

    Within weeks: defense production halts. Consumer electronics manufacturing stops. Within months: wind turbine and EV production ceases. Within a year: the green energy transition reverses. Modern civilization as we know it depends on a group of 17 elements that most people have never heard of. Ask yourself: is this a stable situation?

  • 39 How "dirty" is rare earth mining? +

    Extremely. Processing rare earth ore produces large amounts of toxic and mildly radioactive waste — because rare earth deposits often contain thorium and uranium. In China, this has caused significant environmental damage near mining areas. The green technology revolution is built on a foundation of environmental destruction somewhere else. Every wind turbine blade contains rare earths mined and processed in conditions that would be illegal in the countries using the turbine.

  • 40 Why don't Western countries simply open more mines? +

    Environmental regulations, permitting processes, and community opposition make opening a mine in the US or Europe a decade-long process. A mine approved today won't produce at scale until the mid-2030s. China had no such constraints in the 1980s and 1990s. The regulatory gap is part of how China won.

  • 41 Which country will be the next "China" in rare earths? +

    Australia is the strongest candidate — it has deposits, a stable government, and Western alliances. But to truly challenge China, a country needs not just mines but processing capacity. No country is close to building that at scale yet. The race is wide open — and whoever wins it will have extraordinary leverage for decades. Rare earths are not just minerals. They are the physical foundation of the next world order. The country that controls them controls the future — not with armies, but with chemistry.
